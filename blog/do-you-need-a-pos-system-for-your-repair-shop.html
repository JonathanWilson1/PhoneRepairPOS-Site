Do You Really Need a POS System for Your Repair Shop?

11 Mar 2026 6 min read
← All articles

Short answer: yes — but not necessarily from day one, and not if you're still in the early stages of figuring out whether the business is viable.

Here's a longer, more useful answer.

Most articles about POS systems for repair shops were written by people selling POS systems for repair shops. This one isn't. The goal here is to help you work out whether you actually need one, what it does for a repair business specifically, and what the honest signals are that you've outgrown whatever you're currently using.

What a POS system actually does for a repair shop

In a regular retail environment, a POS system is essentially a till — it processes transactions, tracks inventory, and maybe generates a receipt. That's useful, but it doesn't map neatly to how a repair shop works.

A repair shop POS system is different, because a repair shop's core activity is not selling products — it's taking in devices, doing work, and returning them. That workflow has layers that a standard retail POS doesn't address.

A good repair shop POS system handles:

  • Job intake. Capturing the device details, the reported fault, the agreed price, and the customer's contact information at the point of drop-off.
  • Ticket management. Tracking the status of each repair job — waiting for diagnosis, in progress, waiting for parts, ready for collection — so nothing falls through the cracks.
  • Customer records. Storing who brought in what, what was repaired, how much was charged, and whether they've been in before.
  • Payments. Processing payment at collection and recording what was paid, when, and by what method.
  • Notes and diagnostics. Keeping a record of what was found, what was done, and what the customer was told — which matters enormously if there's ever a dispute.

This is meaningfully different from a till. And none of it works well in a spreadsheet at anything above low volume.

Who doesn't need a POS system yet

If you're doing one or two repairs a week from home, you don't need a POS system. A notebook works. A Google Sheet works. You can hold the status of three open jobs in your head without any system at all.

At this stage, spending time evaluating, setting up, and learning a new software tool costs more than it saves. Your energy is better spent on getting customers, developing your repair skills, and working out what you want to charge.

Similarly, if you're operating as a sole trader with a very simple model — people drop off phones, you fix them, they pay cash — and you're comfortable with very manual record-keeping, you can operate without a POS system indefinitely. It's not ideal, but it works.

Who does need one

The picture changes quickly once the business grows beyond a handful of jobs per week. These are the clearest signals:

You have a shop front. Once you're operating from a fixed location with regular footfall, your intake volume and the expectations of customers both increase. People expect a proper ticket, a realistic turnaround time, and to be notified when their phone is done. Managing this manually is error-prone and time-consuming.

You're doing five or more repairs a day. At this volume, mental tracking stops working. Jobs start blurring together. You'll find yourself checking whether a phone is ready, not being sure, and losing time. A system where every job has a status and you can find any ticket in seconds is worth a lot.

You want to scale. If you want to hire a second person, open a second location, or just stop doing everything yourself, you need systems. A POS is the core of those systems for a repair shop. Without it, you're rebuilding everything from scratch every time something changes.

The "I just use cash and a notebook" objection

This is a real and legitimate approach, and plenty of repair shop owners have run profitable businesses for years this way. The question isn't whether it works — it's whether it continues to work as the business grows, and what happens when something goes wrong.

Cash and a notebook works fine until one of these happens:

  • A customer claims they paid and you have no record proving they didn't
  • A paper ticket gets lost and you're not sure which phone belongs to which customer
  • A job sits forgotten in the back for three weeks because nothing flagged it as overdue
  • You hire someone and they can't read your handwriting or find relevant job history
  • Someone questions what was agreed on a repair and you have no notes to refer to

Any one of these incidents — and they happen in every busy repair shop eventually — changes the calculation. The one-off cost of resolving a dispute or losing a device is often worth more than a year of software subscription fees.

A practical decision checklist

You probably need a POS system if: you're doing 5+ repairs a week, you have or plan to hire staff, you want customer history, you operate from a shop front, or you've already had a dispute or lost a ticket. If none of those apply yet, a spreadsheet is fine for now.

What to look for in a repair shop POS

Once you've decided a POS system makes sense, the choice comes down to a few practical factors.

Is it built for repair shops? Generic retail POS systems are not good fits. The core concept of a job ticket — something that gets created, progresses through stages, and gets closed — is specific to service businesses. Software that understands this workflow will save you time compared to one that treats everything as a simple product sale.

Is it easy to use quickly? You're typically taking in a phone while a customer is standing in front of you. The intake process should be fast. If the software takes five minutes to create a ticket, it's going to create friction and possibly mistakes at the busiest moments.

Does it work on your hardware? Many repair shops use iPads as their counter device. iPad-native apps tend to be faster and more reliable than browser-based tools running on a tablet. It's worth checking whether the software is genuinely built for your device, rather than just accessible from it.

What does it cost? Reasonable repair shop software runs from around £10–£50/month. Be cautious of tools that charge per-ticket fees, which can become expensive at higher volume. A flat monthly fee is usually simpler to budget for.

The honest bottom line

A POS system is not a magic solution. It won't make your repairs faster or your prices more competitive. What it does is reduce admin overhead, give you better records, and make the operational side of running a shop significantly less chaotic as you grow.

If you're past the very early stage, running a shop front, and doing a meaningful number of repairs each day, a repair-specific POS system is almost certainly worth the cost. The question is just which one — and the best approach is to try a few and see which one fits the way you actually work.
