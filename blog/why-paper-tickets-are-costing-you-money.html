Why Paper Tickets Are Costing You Money

4 Mar 2026 4 min read
← All articles

Paper job tickets feel harmless. You've got a stack of them, your handwriting is legible enough, and you've been managing fine. But spend a week honestly tracking what goes wrong — and what it costs you — and the picture changes quickly.

Lost tickets, disputed payments, and time spent hunting for job information are all expenses. They just don't show up on any invoice.

The hidden cost of lost tickets

A phone repair shop taking in ten jobs a day will lose a ticket now and then. It's not negligence — it's physics. Paper gets wet, ends up in a pocket, slides under a workbench, or gets binned by accident along with packaging. When it happens, you're not just losing a bit of card. You're losing:

  • The customer's contact details
  • The agreed repair price
  • Notes on what was found during diagnosis
  • Any special instructions or agreed extras

Now you're ringing the customer from memory, quoting a price you're not sure about, and hoping the repair went the way you think it did. Even if you recover the situation, you've spent 20 minutes doing it — and the customer's confidence in your shop has taken a dent.

At ten jobs a day, losing one ticket a week is a 2% ticket loss rate. That sounds small. But if each ticket represents an average of £40 in labour, that's potentially £80 a month in jobs where something goes wrong because the information disappeared.

Illegible handwriting and miscommunication

You might write clearly. But does everyone who works in your shop? And how clear is your own writing when you're taking in three jobs at once, a customer's talking to you, and someone on the phone wants a quote?

Misread handwriting leads to real problems:

  • A technician orders the wrong part because the model number wasn't clear
  • A customer gets quoted £45 but is charged £65 because someone misread the ticket
  • A repair is done on the wrong fault because the description was ambiguous

A single wrongly ordered part can cost more than a month's worth of any digital system. And the customer still has to wait while the right part arrives.

Digital intake forms eliminate this entirely. The customer's name, device, and fault description are typed once and readable by anyone. There's no ambiguity about whether that's a 6 or a b, whether it says "screen" or "rear".

No payment trail means disputes go against you

This is the one that costs shops the most and gets talked about the least.

A customer returns two weeks after collecting their device and says they never paid, or that they were quoted less. Without a clear record — amount, method, date, and ideally a signature — you have nothing concrete to point to. Most shops in this situation end up eating the loss just to avoid the confrontation.

Paper till rolls tell you the daily total was correct. They don't tell you which job each payment was for, what method was used, or whether a deposit was taken. That gap is exactly where disputes live.

Real scenario

A customer drops in a damaged iPhone 14, agrees to a £90 screen replacement, and pays a £30 deposit in cash. Three weeks later they claim they never agreed to £90 and never paid any deposit. If your only record is a paper ticket you can barely read, good luck proving otherwise.

Time wasted searching for job information

Ask yourself honestly: how long does it take to find a specific job when a customer calls asking for an update? If the answer is anything more than about ten seconds, you're losing time every single day.

Flipping through a stack of tickets to find the one with the right name. Checking the pile of "done" phones at the back. Asking your colleague if they know where the Samsung Galaxy is. These are small delays individually, but they add up to real time — and they happen in front of customers, which affects the impression your shop makes.

In a busy shop, handling even five status enquiries a day, each taking two minutes to resolve instead of ten seconds, costs you roughly 70 minutes a week. That's time you could spend on actual repairs.

No visibility means no oversight

Paper tickets only exist in one place. If you're not physically in the shop, you have no idea how many jobs are in, which are overdue, or whether a customer has been waiting three days for a callback.

That matters when you take a day off, when you have a second location, or when you want to see at the end of the month how many repairs came through. With paper, the answer is to count tickets — assuming they haven't been thrown away yet.

Digital systems give you a running count of open jobs, overdue repairs, and completed work — without any extra effort. The record is just there, because it was created when the job was booked in.

What going digital actually solves

Every problem above has a straightforward digital solution:

  • Lost tickets: gone entirely — jobs live in the system until you explicitly close them
  • Illegible handwriting: typed fields, consistent formats, no ambiguity
  • Payment disputes: every payment is logged against the job, with method and date
  • Slow status updates: search by name or device in seconds
  • No oversight: see all open jobs from anywhere, any time

The upfront concern is usually change — learning a new system, getting staff to use it consistently, dealing with the transition period. These are real considerations. But they're one-time costs, and the ongoing savings in time, disputes, and lost jobs compound from day one.

A word on cost

Some shop owners assume going digital is expensive. In practice, the cost of a simple digital job management app is a fraction of what a single lost ticket or disputed payment costs you. If you're taking in 50+ repairs a week, the time saving alone pays for it within days.

The more relevant question isn't "can I afford a digital system?" It's "how much has my paper system already cost me this month?"

Making the switch

You don't need to change everything at once. A sensible approach is to run digital intake alongside your existing process for a week — use both in parallel, then drop the paper once you're comfortable. Most shops find they stop missing it within a few days.
