A Straightforward Guide to Setting Repair Prices

7 Mar 2026 7 min read
← All articles

Pricing is one of the things repair shop owners get wrong most often — not because they're bad at business, but because they've never sat down and worked out the actual numbers. Most people price by gut feel, by what the shop down the road charges, or by what they think a customer will pay. None of those methods reliably produces a price that keeps the business alive and profitable.

This guide walks through how to build a repair price from the ground up, avoid the most common mistakes, and raise prices without losing customers.

Start With Your Costs, Not Your Competitors

The first thing to understand is that your pricing problem is a costs problem in disguise. If you don't know what a repair costs you to deliver, you can't price it correctly — no matter what your competitors charge.

Every repair has three cost components:

  1. Parts cost: what you paid for the component (screen, battery, charging port, etc.) including any shipping
  2. Labour cost: the time the repair takes, multiplied by your effective hourly rate
  3. Overhead allocation: your share of fixed costs — rent, insurance, software, utilities — allocated per job

Let's take each in turn.

Calculating Parts Cost

Parts cost is the most obvious component, but people still get it wrong. Common mistakes: forgetting to include shipping (if you're ordering small quantities, carriage per unit can be £2–£5), not accounting for dead-on-arrival rates (some parts fail on first fit — the cost of that is built into your pricing), and using the cheapest possible parts without factoring in the warranty risk.

If you use aftermarket parts, your parts cost is lower, but you may face more comebacks. If you use OEM or high-quality equivalents, cost is higher but warranty claims are rarer. Both approaches can work — the key is that your price reflects which route you've taken.

Calculating Labour Cost

To price labour, you need an hourly rate. Start by working out what you want to earn. If you want to take home £35,000/year as a sole trader, that's roughly £675/week. If you work 45 hours/week, your effective rate needs to be at least £15/hour just to cover your own wage — before any business costs.

A realistic labour rate for a UK repair shop, including salary and employer costs, is £25–£40/hour depending on the operator and location. If you're in a city, lean towards the top of that range. If you're in a lower-cost area, £25–£30/hour is still competitive and sustainable.

Time each common repair a few times and use the average. An iPhone screen replacement takes 20–35 minutes for most technicians. At £30/hour, that's £10–£17.50 in labour. Use the average across several jobs, not your fastest time.

Overhead Allocation

This is the number most shop owners ignore, and it's the one that slowly bleeds profit. Add up your monthly fixed costs: rent, business rates, insurance, broadband, software subscriptions, card machine fees (the fixed element), consumables, and tools. Let's say that totals £1,500/month. Divide by the number of jobs you complete each month. If you complete 100 repairs/month, your overhead per job is £15.

That £15 needs to be in every price you quote, or you're subsidising your business from your own wage. Most repair shops that struggle aren't doing bad work — they're doing good work at prices that don't cover what it costs to run the business.

Worked example: iPhone 14 screen replacement

Parts (aftermarket screen, inc. shipping): £42  |  Labour (45 min at £30/hr): £22.50  |  Overhead allocation: £15  |  Total cost: £79.50

To hit a 25% net margin, add £26.50 — giving a target price of £106. Rounding to £99 or £105 is a reasonable commercial decision. Going below £90 means you're either working below cost or paying yourself less than minimum wage.

Common Pricing Mistakes

Underpricing to win work

Charging £49 for an iPhone screen replacement because the shop up the road does it for £55 doesn't make you competitive — it makes you unprofitable. Customers who choose purely on price are the least loyal customers you have. The customer who pays £90 for a quality repair with a 90-day warranty is far more likely to return than the one you charged £49, who will still complain if anything goes wrong.

The race to the bottom

When everyone in a local area keeps undercutting each other, prices fall below sustainable levels and everyone loses. The way out is to compete on quality and trust, not price. Be transparent about the parts you use, offer a clear warranty, communicate well, and charge what the service is worth. Plenty of customers value this over saving £10 — and those are the customers worth having.

Not updating prices as costs rise

Parts prices change. Supplier shipping costs go up. Rent increases. If you set your prices in 2023 and haven't reviewed them since, the chances are your margins have been quietly shrinking. Review your cost base at least once a year and adjust prices accordingly. A small annual increase is much easier for customers to accept than a large jump after years of no change.

Researching Competitor Pricing

Knowing what competitors charge is useful context — but it shouldn't set your floor. The right way to use competitor data is to understand the market range, position yourself within it, and then make sure that position is profitable for you.

Check Google for "[your town] phone repair" and visit the top 5 results. Many shops list their prices on their website or Google Business Profile. Note what they charge for the three or four most common repairs. If you're consistently £15–£20 above the local average, that's fine — but be ready to explain why (better parts, longer warranty, faster turnaround, better customer experience).

If a competitor is charging prices you genuinely cannot match without losing money, let them have those customers. Chasing every low-margin job is not a growth strategy.

OEM vs Aftermarket: Tiered Pricing

Many shops offer two price points for the same repair: an aftermarket option and an OEM-grade option. This works well because it gives customers a choice, and it gives you a higher-margin tier to offer.

A common structure:

  • Aftermarket screen: standard price, 30-day warranty
  • OEM-grade or genuine Apple screen: £20–£40 more, 90-day warranty

Be clear with customers about what they're getting. When you explain the difference honestly — part quality, warranty length, how the display looks — most customers make an informed choice and don't resent it. The ones who choose the premium tier rarely complain; they know what they paid for.

Deposit Policy

For high-value or unusual repairs — older devices, rare parts, complex water damage, anything where the parts are non-returnable — take a deposit at intake. A 30–50% deposit covers your parts cost if the customer doesn't collect, and it signals commitment on both sides. Most customers accept a deposit request as professional and reasonable. State your deposit policy clearly when quoting and include it on the job ticket so there's no ambiguity later.

How to Raise Prices Without Losing Customers

The fear of raising prices is usually worse than the reality. Customers are less price-sensitive than repair shop owners tend to assume. Here's how to do it cleanly:

  • Don't apologise: announce the change matter-of-factly. "Our prices have been updated as of [date]." No lengthy explanation needed. Apologising signals that you think the new price is wrong.
  • Raise gradually: a £10 increase is easier for everyone to absorb than a sudden £25 jump. Incremental changes spread over two reviews are less noticeable.
  • Raise on new quotes, not mid-job: never change a price after you've quoted a customer. Stick to your quote; update the price list for the next person.
  • Lead with value: if you're raising prices, make sure your service quality justifies it. Improve turnaround time, tighten your warranty, or add small extras like a screen clean on collection.

Customers who leave because your price went from £75 to £85 were probably not the loyal customers driving your repeat business. Holding a sustainable price point keeps the business healthy and lets you provide a consistently better service to the customers who value quality over the cheapest option.
