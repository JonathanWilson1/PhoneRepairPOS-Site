A Straightforward Guide to Setting Repair Prices

18 Feb 2026 5 min read
← All articles

Pricing is one of the most awkward parts of running a repair shop. Set your prices too low and you're working hard for margins that don't cover your costs. Set them too high and customers walk past your door to the shop down the road. Get it right and pricing almost runs itself — you quote confidently, customers accept, and the numbers add up at the end of the month.

The good news is that pricing phone repairs is more science than art. There's a clear framework for working out what to charge, and once you've done it once, updating prices as costs change is straightforward.

Why pricing is genuinely hard

The challenge with repair pricing isn't laziness — it's that three variables are always moving at once. Parts costs fluctuate depending on your supplier, the device model, and whether you're using OEM, aftermarket, or refurbished components. Time varies because two "identical" screen replacements can take very different amounts of work depending on the condition of the device. And competition is local and highly visible: customers often know within a few pounds what the shop across the road is charging.

Shops that don't have a pricing framework tend to do one of two things: price on gut feel (which usually means undercharging) or price to match competitors (which means inheriting their mistakes). Neither approach is sustainable.

Pricing isn't just about winning the job. It's about winning the job at a number that makes running your business worth it.

Cost-plus pricing: the foundation

The most reliable starting point for any repair price is cost-plus pricing. The formula is simple:

Price = Parts cost + Labour cost + Overhead allocation + Target margin

Work through each component in turn.

Parts cost

This is what you actually paid for the component — not what the supplier lists, but what appears on your invoice including VAT and shipping. If you bought a batch of ten screens and one was faulty, spread the cost of the faulty one across the other nine. Your effective parts cost per repair is always slightly higher than the unit price suggests.

Labour cost

Work out your hourly rate by dividing your target monthly earnings by the number of billable hours you can realistically work. If you want to earn £2,500 a month and you can complete roughly 100 billable hours of repair work, your labour rate is £25 per hour. A 20-minute screen replacement costs you roughly £8 in labour. A 90-minute logic board repair costs £37.50.

Overhead allocation

Rent, utilities, insurance, software subscriptions, tools — these costs exist regardless of how many repairs you do. Divide your monthly overhead by the number of jobs you complete in a typical month. If your overhead is £600 and you complete 80 jobs, you need to recover £7.50 per job just to break even on costs.

Margin

Add a margin on top — typically 20–40% depending on your market. This is your profit, your buffer for slow months, and your fund for buying better tools or training.

What to charge for common repairs

These are approximate ranges based on typical UK market pricing in early 2026. Your numbers will vary depending on your parts supplier and local competition.

  • Screen replacements (iPhone 13/14/15): £70–£120 depending on model and screen grade
  • Battery replacement: £25–£50 depending on model complexity
  • Charging port repair: £35–£65 — labour-intensive on some models
  • Diagnostics fee: £15–£25, often waived if the repair goes ahead
  • Camera replacement: £40–£80 depending on module type
  • Water damage assessment: £30–£50, with no guarantee of successful repair

If your cost-plus calculation is coming out lower than these ranges, you're either getting excellent parts prices or you're undervaluing your labour. If it's coming out higher, check whether your overhead allocation is accurate — many shops overestimate how much overhead to assign to each job.

Common mistake

Quoting parts cost plus a flat £10 for labour is not a pricing strategy. It ignores overhead entirely and usually results in prices that feel right but are quietly losing money on every job.

Competitor research without a race to the bottom

Checking what local competitors charge is sensible — but the goal isn't to match or undercut them. The goal is to understand the market so you can position yourself within it.

Do a simple audit once or twice a year: look up three or four local repair shops and note their prices for screen replacements and battery swaps on the most common iPhone models. This gives you a range. If your cost-plus price sits comfortably within that range, you're in good shape. If it's significantly higher, investigate whether you can reduce parts costs through a different supplier. If it's significantly lower, you may be undercharging.

The shops that win on price alone attract customers who will leave the moment someone cheaper opens nearby. The shops that win on service, speed, and trust can charge at the higher end of the local range and retain customers. Focus on the latter.

When to adjust your prices

Prices aren't set-and-forget. Review them whenever:

  • Your parts supplier changes their prices (up or down)
  • A new device model comes out and existing parts prices drop
  • Your overhead costs change — new rent, new insurance premium
  • You're consistently turning away work because you're too busy (a sign you could charge more)
  • You're consistently losing quotes to competitors (a sign you may be too high, or need to communicate value better)

You can also adjust prices situationally. Loyal customers who bring you five phones a year might get a small discount. Jobs that arrive on a Saturday afternoon when you're slammed might carry a premium. Complex repairs on older devices where there's a real risk of failure warrant a higher quoted price with a clear explanation of why.

Recording what you actually charged

One thing that catches many repair shops out is not knowing what they've actually charged historically. If every job is a verbal quote that you write on a paper ticket, you have no easy way to review whether your pricing is working — you can't see average job values, you can't spot which repair types are most profitable, and you can't quickly update a price across all your standard jobs.

Pricing doesn't need to be complicated. Build your numbers from the ground up, sense-check against your market, and review when costs change. Do that, and you'll stop leaving money on the table.
