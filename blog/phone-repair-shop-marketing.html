Marketing Your Repair Shop on a Budget

14 Feb 2026 6 min read
← All articles

Most phone repair shops don't need a marketing agency. They need customers to find them when their screen cracks — and that's a very solvable problem. The channels that consistently deliver walk-ins for repair shops are almost entirely free to use, and the paid options that work are cheap when used correctly.

This guide focuses on what actually moves the needle for an independent repair shop with limited time and a limited budget.

Google Business Profile: your most important asset

When someone in your town searches "phone repair near me", the results they see first aren't websites — they're Google Business Profile listings. That map pack at the top of the search results is where most of your potential customers are making their decision. If you're not in it, or if your listing is incomplete, you're losing jobs to competitors every day.

Setting up and optimising your Google Business Profile is free and takes about an hour. Here's what to focus on:

  • Complete every field. Business name, address, phone number, website, opening hours, and services. The more complete your listing, the better Google ranks it.
  • Choose the right primary category. "Mobile Phone Repair Shop" is usually the correct primary category. You can add secondary categories for tablet repair, computer repair, and so on.
  • Add photos. The exterior of your shop, your workspace, your tools — real photos outperform stock images. Listings with photos get significantly more clicks.
  • Post updates regularly. Google lets you post offers, news, and updates directly to your listing. Posting once a fortnight signals to Google that your listing is active and maintained.
  • Answer questions. If someone asks a question on your listing, answer it. Unanswered questions get answered by random members of the public, which is not ideal.

Local SEO basics

Beyond your Google Business Profile, local SEO is about making sure your website and online presence clearly communicate where you are and what you do. Search engines need consistent, clear signals to rank a local business confidently.

The three things that matter most for local SEO are:

Location keywords on your website

Your website should mention your town and nearby areas naturally throughout the content. Not stuffed awkwardly into every sentence, but clearly present on your homepage, your about page, and any service pages. "iPhone screen repair in Manchester" is a phrase worth having on your site. "We fix phones" is not enough.

Consistent NAP across the web

NAP stands for Name, Address, Phone number. Wherever your business is listed online — Google, Yelp, Yell.com, local directories — these three details should be identical. Inconsistencies confuse search engines and dilute your local ranking signal. Do a quick search for your business name and correct any listings where the details differ from your Google Business Profile.

A website that loads fast on mobile

Most people searching for a repair shop are doing it on the cracked phone they need fixing. If your website takes five seconds to load on a mobile connection, a significant number of them will go back and click a competitor instead. Use Google's PageSpeed Insights to check your site speed and address any glaring issues.

Review generation strategy

Reviews are the single biggest trust signal for a local business. A shop with 80 reviews averaging 4.7 stars will beat a shop with 12 reviews averaging 4.9 every time, simply because the volume of reviews is more convincing than a perfect score.

The most effective time to ask for a review is at the moment of collection — when the customer has their phone back, it's working perfectly, and they're relieved and happy. That emotional peak is when they're most likely to follow through.

Keep it simple: say something like "It would really help us if you left a quick Google review — it only takes a minute." Have a QR code printed at the counter that links directly to your review page so there's no friction. Don't ask via email two days later — the moment has passed.

Target

Aim to ask every customer who collects a completed repair. Even if only one in five leaves a review, you'll accumulate reviews faster than almost any competitor who isn't actively asking.

Respond to every review — positive and negative. A gracious response to a critical review often impresses prospective customers more than the negative review itself damages you. It shows professionalism and accountability.

Social media for repair shops

Social media is one area where it's easy to waste time. Let's be direct about what works and what doesn't.

What tends not to work: elaborate video productions, daily posting schedules you can't sustain, chasing followers, trying to go viral. These consume time and produce minimal walk-in customers for a local repair shop.

What can work: a Facebook page with your opening hours and contact details up to date (many people check Facebook before calling), occasional posts showing a satisfying before/after repair, and responding quickly to direct messages. If someone messages your Facebook page asking your prices, a fast response converts them. A response the next day loses them.

Instagram can work well if you're willing to photograph your work consistently — water damage repairs, heavily cracked screens restored to perfect, satisfying tool photos. This builds a local audience over time and demonstrates craft. But it requires consistency, and sporadic posting is worse than no posting at all because it signals an abandoned account.

If you're choosing one social channel to maintain, make it Facebook. The demographic of people breaking phones skews older than you might think, and Facebook remains where they go to find and evaluate local businesses.

Word of mouth: how to systematise referrals

Word of mouth is the highest-quality lead source for a repair shop. A customer who was referred by a friend arrives already trusting you, is less likely to haggle, and is more likely to return. The challenge is that word of mouth feels like it happens randomly — some customers recommend you, most don't.

You can make it less random. A few approaches that work:

  • Ask directly. When a customer picks up their phone, tell them: "If you know anyone who needs a repair, please send them our way." It sounds obvious, but most shops never say it.
  • Business cards. Give every customer two business cards when they collect. One for them, one to pass on. This is a £20 investment that keeps working.
  • Referral offer. A simple "refer a friend and get £5 off your next repair" creates a real incentive to recommend you. Track it by asking new customers how they heard about you.
  • Corporate accounts. Local offices, small businesses, and schools all have devices that break. Introduce yourself, offer a small discount for volume, and become their go-to repair shop. One corporate relationship can be worth dozens of individual customers.

Budget allocation: free first, then paid

If you're starting out or working with minimal budget, the priority order is:

  1. Google Business Profile — free, highest ROI
  2. Review generation — free, compounds over time
  3. Website local SEO — free or low cost
  4. Facebook page maintenance — free
  5. Business cards and printed materials — low cost, high impact

Once the free channels are working, paid options worth testing include Google Local Services Ads (you pay per lead, not per click) and boosted Facebook posts targeting people within a few miles of your shop. Both can deliver strong returns for a repair shop, but only once your listing and reviews are already in good shape — paid traffic sent to a weak listing or a shop with few reviews will underperform.

The shops that consistently win on marketing aren't spending more — they're showing up in the right places, asking for reviews, and treating every customer as a potential referral source. That costs almost nothing and compounds every week.
