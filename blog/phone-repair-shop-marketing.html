Marketing Your Repair Shop on a Budget

8 Mar 2026 6 min read
← All articles

Most phone repair shop owners didn't go into business to do marketing. They went in because they're good at fixing phones. But without customers, none of that skill matters. The good news: for a local repair shop, the most effective marketing costs almost nothing. It just takes consistency.

This is a list of things that actually work — no paid ads, no social media agency, no jargon. Just tactics a one-person or small-team shop can act on this week.

Google Business Profile: The Single Most Important Thing You Can Do

When someone's screen cracks, the first thing they do is search "phone repair near me" on Google. If your business doesn't appear in those results — or appears without reviews, photos, or accurate details — you lose that customer before they've even considered you.

Setting up and optimising a Google Business Profile is free and takes about an hour. Here's what matters:

  • Business name and category: use "Phone Repair Shop" as your primary category. Add secondary categories like "Mobile Phone Accessories" if relevant.
  • Opening hours: keep these accurate. Wrong hours = frustrated customers and bad reviews.
  • Phone number and address: obvious, but double-check they're correct.
  • Services: list specific repairs — screen replacement, battery replacement, water damage, charging port repair. Google surfaces this in search results.
  • Photos: add photos of your shopfront, your workspace, and completed repairs. Listings with photos get significantly more clicks than those without.
  • Posts: Google lets you post updates, offers, and announcements. A monthly post keeps the listing active and signals to Google that the business is current.

Once it's set up, reviews are the thing that makes the biggest difference to how prominently you appear. Which brings us to the next point.

Asking for Reviews (and Doing It Right)

Most happy customers don't leave reviews unless they're asked. Most unhappy customers do, without being asked. This means that if you're passive about reviews, your rating will be dragged down by the small minority of difficult jobs — while the majority of satisfied customers leave no trace.

The fix is simple: ask. When a customer collects their device and everything went well, say: "If you've got a minute, it would really help us if you left a Google review." Then show them the QR code that links directly to your review page — you can generate this from Google Business Profile for free.

Timing matters. Ask when the customer is happy, not when they're in a rush or if there was any friction. A satisfied customer who leaves in a good mood is your most reliable source of new business.

Aim for 20+ reviews with a 4.5+ average before you worry about anything else. That alone puts most local repair shops ahead of most of their competition.

Referral Incentives

Word of mouth is the oldest form of marketing and still one of the most effective for local service businesses. You can make it slightly more systematic without it feeling manufactured.

A simple referral scheme: give customers a small card at collection that says "Refer a friend — they get £5 off their first repair, you get £5 off your next one." Print 100 of these at a local print shop for about £8. Hand them out with every completed job. The people most likely to refer are those who just had a good experience — which is exactly when you're handing over the device.

Keep the incentive modest. You're not trying to run a complex loyalty programme. You just want existing customers to mention your name when their friends or colleagues crack a screen.

Local Facebook Groups

Almost every town and neighbourhood has at least one active Facebook group — "X Town Community", "Things to do in X", "Buy/Sell/Swap X". These groups often have thousands of members and near-daily activity.

Join the relevant local groups. Don't spam them with ads — that gets you removed. Instead, be useful. If someone posts "can anyone recommend a phone repair shop in [town]?", that's your moment to respond professionally and offer to help. One genuine reply in a busy thread can drive 5–10 new enquiries.

You can also occasionally post legitimately — if you've just started doing a new repair type, or you have a special offer running, a brief post in a community group is usually welcome if it's not done every week.

Leafleting in the Right Places

Leaflets feel old-fashioned, but for a local repair shop they work — especially if you target them well. The highest-value locations:

  • Near phone network shops: people who've just been quoted £300 for a screen replacement by a network provider walk past your leaflet and see "£69 screen repair, same day". That's a compelling contrast.
  • Near schools and colleges: young people break phones at a high rate and often pay themselves. A simple A6 card with your prices and address, pinned to a community board near a school, costs pennies and can generate steady enquiries.
  • Nearby businesses: office blocks, retail units, gyms. A stack of business cards or small flyers left with the reception desk can work well, especially in places with lots of footfall.

Don't bother with mass mail drops or door-to-door leafleting to residential streets — the response rate is too low to justify the cost. Target places where the people are already thinking about their phones.

Simple Social Media: Before and After Photos

You don't need a social media strategy. You don't need a content calendar. But a basic Instagram or Facebook page with occasional before-and-after photos serves two purposes: it proves you do real work, and it gives people something to share.

The format is simple. Cracked screen comes in — take a photo before you start. Repaired device ready to go — take a photo after. Post both together. That's the whole thing. "iPhone 15 screen replacement — in and out in 45 minutes." No hashtag strategy required.

Do this 2–3 times a week and within a few months you have a visible portfolio of your work. When a potential customer checks you out online before calling, they see real evidence of quality. That builds trust before they've spoken to you.

Partnerships With Local Businesses

Phone shops and network provider stores (EE, Vodafone, O2 independents) get customers asking about repairs every day, and they typically don't do them. An informal arrangement — "if you send customers our way, we'll send accessories enquiries to you" — can generate steady referrals at zero cost.

Other good partnership targets: phone case and accessories shops, secondhand phone dealers, computer repair shops (they often get phone enquiries), and pawn shops. Walk in, introduce yourself, leave a stack of business cards. Many will be happy to refer if they know you exist and trust the quality of your work.

The marketing priority order

If you're starting from scratch: (1) set up and fully complete your Google Business Profile, (2) start asking for reviews after every successful job, (3) set up a basic social media page and post before/afters. Once those three things are consistent, everything else is a bonus.

Loyalty Schemes

A simple loyalty card — "5 repairs, get £10 off your next one" — gives regular customers a reason to come back to you rather than trying a competitor. Print these cheaply, hand them out at collection, and stamp them each visit.

Don't overthink it. The value isn't in the discount — most customers won't redeem it. The value is in the feeling of being a regular customer. People like being loyal to a local business they trust. A loyalty card is just a small signal that you recognise and reward that loyalty.

These tactics taken together — Google Business, reviews, referrals, local presence, and simple social content — are enough to build a steady flow of local customers without spending money on advertising. The shops that grow fastest are usually not the ones with the best marketing budget. They're the ones that do good work, ask for reviews, and stay visible in their community.
