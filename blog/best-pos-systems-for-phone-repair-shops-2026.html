Best POS Systems for Phone Repair Shops in 2026

12 Mar 2026 7 min read
← All articles

Choosing the right point-of-sale system for a phone repair shop is harder than it looks. Most POS software is built for retail — stock scanning, till reconciliation, end-of-day reports. Repair shops need something different: job tickets, status tracking, customer communications, and a clear record of who paid what and when.

This guide looks at the main options available in 2026, what they actually do well, where they fall short, and who each one suits best. The goal is to give you enough information to make a sensible decision — not to push any particular product.

What makes a POS good for repair shops?

Before diving into specific products, it helps to know what you're actually looking for. General retail POS systems can handle payments, but they rarely handle the workflow that makes a repair shop tick. Here's what matters:

  • Ticket or job management — the ability to create a record for each repair, attach it to a customer, and track it from intake to collection
  • Status tracking — moving a job through stages (Received, Diagnosing, Repairing, Ready) so you and your team always know what's happening
  • Payment recording — logging what was paid, by what method, and when — linked directly to the job
  • Customer records — keeping a history of previous repairs for each customer, which is useful for recurring issues and for resolving disputes
  • Offline capability — repair shops can't afford to lose access to their ticket list because the internet drops

Secondary features — inventory management, invoicing, parts ordering, multi-location support — are useful for larger or more complex operations, but they're not essential when you're just starting out or running a tight single-location shop.

RepairDesk

RepairDesk is one of the most established names in repair shop software. It's a cloud-based platform that covers a wide range of features: ticket management, inventory, invoicing, parts ordering, customer-facing notifications, and detailed reporting. It supports multiple locations and multiple technicians, and integrates with payment processors and accounting software.

The pricing is subscription-based, starting at around $75–$125 per month depending on the plan and whether you're billed monthly or annually. For a shop doing significant volume, that cost is easy to justify. For a sole operator just getting started, it's a meaningful outgoing.

Because RepairDesk is cloud-based, it requires a reliable internet connection. If your broadband drops, you lose access to your tickets. Setup takes some time — there are configuration options to work through and staff training involved. But for shops that need everything in one place and are willing to invest in setup, it's a capable platform.

RepairShopr

RepairShopr (now part of Syncro) is another full-featured cloud platform, historically popular in the US market. It combines repair ticketing with CRM, invoicing, and marketing tools. Like RepairDesk, it's subscription-based and aimed at established businesses rather than shops just getting started.

The feature set is broad — perhaps broader than most repair shops need. If you're also doing computer repair, IT support, or other service-based work, the all-in-one approach makes more sense. For a phone-only repair shop, you may find yourself paying for a lot of functionality you never open.

Square

Square is a general-purpose POS and payment platform used by businesses across many industries. It handles card payments, has a simple register interface, and offers some appointment and job management features through its broader ecosystem.

The core product is free to use (you pay per transaction), which makes it appealing. But Square isn't built for repair shops. There's no native concept of a job ticket, no status stages, and no way to tie a payment to a specific repair job out of the box. You can cobble together a workflow using notes and custom fields, but it takes effort and it's not how the product was designed to work.

Square is a reasonable choice if you're primarily looking for a card payment terminal and don't need structured job tracking. For anything more, you'll find yourself working around its limitations.

PhoneRepairPOS

PhoneRepairPOS is an iPad-first app built specifically for phone repair shops. It's free to download on the App Store. The focus is deliberately narrow: create jobs, track status, record payments. There's no subscription, no configuration maze, and no features you'll never use.

It works offline using iCloud sync, so your ticket list is always available regardless of internet connectivity. Setup takes minutes rather than hours. It suits sole operators and small shops that want a clean, organised system without committing to a monthly software bill.

The trade-off is scope. PhoneRepairPOS doesn't have inventory management, invoicing, parts ordering, or multi-location support. If those things are important to your operation, a more comprehensive platform like RepairDesk may be a better fit.

Who each option suits best

RepairDesk — best for growing or multi-location shops

If you're managing multiple technicians, multiple locations, significant parts inventory, or you need proper invoicing and accounting integration, RepairDesk is the most fully-featured option available. The monthly cost is justified by the breadth of what it does.

RepairShopr — best for mixed repair businesses

If your shop does phone repair alongside computer repair, IT support, or other service work, RepairShopr's broad feature set makes sense. It's built to handle varied service businesses under one roof.

Square — best for payment-first operations

If you already have a system for tracking jobs and just need a clean card payment solution, Square is a reasonable choice. Don't expect it to manage your repair workflow.

PhoneRepairPOS — best for solo operators and small shops

If you're running a single-location shop, working alone or with one other technician, and you want something you can be up and running with in minutes — PhoneRepairPOS is worth trying. It's free, works offline, and does exactly what a repair shop needs without anything it doesn't.

Making the right choice

The honest answer is that the best POS system is the one you'll actually use consistently. A powerful platform that's too complicated to set up properly won't help you. A simple app that covers your core needs and gets used every day will.

Think about where your shop is right now. If you're early-stage or running lean, start simple and upgrade when your operation genuinely demands it. If you're already at volume and losing track of jobs or payments, investing in a more comprehensive platform is probably worth it.

Whatever you choose, the most important thing is having a consistent system — one that every repair goes through, every time. The tool matters less than the habit.
