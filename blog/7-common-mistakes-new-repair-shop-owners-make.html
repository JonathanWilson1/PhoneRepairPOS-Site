7 Common Mistakes New Repair Shop Owners Make

24 Feb 2026 6 min read
← All articles

Starting a phone repair business is exciting. The overheads are low, demand is steady, and the skills are learnable. But there are patterns that catch nearly every new shop owner out — often within the first six months. Here are the seven most common, and how to sidestep them.

1. Underpricing repairs

It's the most consistent mistake new repair shops make. You're not confident yet, so you price low to compete. The problem is that underpricing isn't just a margin issue — it's a positioning issue. Customers associate low prices with low quality. A quote that's dramatically cheaper than everyone else doesn't attract bargain-hunters; it raises suspicion.

More practically: when you underprice, you're often not accounting for your time properly. A screen replacement that takes 45 minutes, requires £30 of parts, and gets charged out at £40 is effectively paying you less than minimum wage after overheads. Multiply that across a full day and you've worked hard for very little.

The fix: price to cover parts + labour at a real hourly rate + a contribution to overheads, and then benchmark against local competitors. Don't be the cheapest. Be fair and competitive.

2. No written ticket or record of the repair

Running repairs from memory or loose notes is manageable when you're doing two jobs a day. It becomes a serious problem at ten. And when a customer disputes a repair — claims the screen was cracked when you handed it back, or insists they paid in cash — your recollection counts for nothing without a written record.

A proper ticket captures what was agreed, what was done, and when payment was taken. It protects you and sets professional expectations with the customer from the moment they hand over the device. This doesn't need to be complex: a printed form, a carbon copy book, or a digital system all work. What doesn't work is nothing.

A dispute with no written record almost always goes in the customer's favour — even if you're right.

3. Ignoring online reviews

For a local repair shop, Google reviews are the single most powerful marketing tool you have — and they're free. When someone searches "phone repair near me", the first thing they see is a map with businesses and star ratings. A shop with 4.8 stars and 50 reviews will win walk-ins over a competitor with no reviews every time, even if the competitor is closer.

New shop owners often don't ask for reviews because it feels awkward. But customers who are happy with a repair are usually glad to leave one — they just need to be prompted. At the end of a repair, when the customer is satisfied, say: "If you've got a moment, a Google review would really help us out." Most will do it.

The compounding effect is significant. A shop that collects two reviews a week will have over 100 within a year. That social proof closes customers before they've even walked in the door.

4. Poor communication with customers

Customers drop off their most important device and then hear nothing. No update when the diagnosis is done. No message when the part arrives. No call when there's a delay. From the customer's perspective, their phone has disappeared into a black hole.

This leads to anxious customers calling the shop repeatedly, which interrupts your work. It also leads to reviews that say "good repair but communication was poor" — which is entirely avoidable.

Set a simple rule: contact the customer at every transition. When diagnosis is complete, call with the quote and get approval. When the repair is done, text to say it's ready. If there's going to be a delay, message before the original estimated time, not after. Proactive communication costs almost nothing and has a dramatic effect on customer satisfaction.

5. Taking on repairs you can't complete

Saying yes to every repair feels like good business. In practice, it leads to devices sitting on your bench for weeks, customers chasing you, and jobs you eventually have to admit you can't fix — potentially after you've caused further damage attempting them.

Know your limits clearly. If a repair is beyond your current skill level — motherboard-level fault finding, micro-soldering, water damage with severe corrosion — say so upfront. Customers respect honesty. "I can't fix this, but I can refer you to someone who can" is far better than "I've been trying for two weeks and I've made it worse."

As your skills develop, your range expands. But at the start, it's better to do fewer repairs reliably than to take on everything and deliver some poorly.

6. No payment records

Cash is still common in phone repair shops, and that's fine. The problem is cash without records. When every payment is a note in a till and nothing is tracked against the specific job, you lose the ability to reconcile your income, spot if money is missing, or demonstrate what was paid in a dispute.

Every payment — cash or card — should be recorded against the repair ticket. Note the amount, the method, and the date. This becomes your payment ledger. At the end of the week you should be able to sum up your recorded payments and have them match what's in your till and bank account. If they don't, something has gone wrong.

This is also important for your tax records. HMRC expects you to be able to demonstrate your income. "I remember roughly how much came in" is not a tax return.

7. Skipping insurance

It feels like an unnecessary expense when you're just starting out and every pound matters. But consider what you're taking on: you're handling customers' personal devices worth up to £1,200. If you drop one, if there's a fire in your workspace, if a customer claims their data was accessed — without insurance, the cost comes out of your pocket.

At minimum, get public liability insurance and goods-in-custody cover (which protects customer devices while they're with you). The combined cost for a sole trader is typically £10–£20 per month. Compare that against the cost of replacing an iPhone Pro Max you accidentally stepped on.

Insurance tip

Mention to customers that their device is insured while in your care. It's a genuine trust signal — and most competitors won't be saying it.

The common thread

Most of these mistakes come down to treating the business casually when it deserves to be run professionally from the start. That doesn't mean bureaucracy — it means having a system for your tickets, your pricing, your communications, and your records. The repair shops that grow are the ones customers trust, and trust comes from doing the basics consistently well.
