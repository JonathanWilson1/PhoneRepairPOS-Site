7 Common Mistakes New Repair Shop Owners Make

12 Mar 2026 7 min read
← All articles

Starting a phone repair shop is one of the more accessible routes into running your own business. The startup costs are relatively low, the skills are learnable, and there's consistent demand. But the early stages are where a lot of shops quietly set themselves up for problems that only become visible months later.

These seven mistakes come up again and again. Most of them are fixable once you know to look for them.

1. Underpricing repairs

This is the most common mistake, and it's driven by a fear that's completely understandable: if you charge too much, customers will go somewhere cheaper. So new owners price at or below what the competition charges, sometimes below what the parts actually cost once you factor in time.

The problem is that underpricing doesn't build a sustainable business — it builds a very busy one that doesn't make money. A phone repair shop with low prices will always have customers. It won't necessarily have profit.

The fix is to price based on your actual costs (parts plus your time at a realistic hourly rate) plus a margin that makes the business viable. If that price is higher than what a competitor charges for a budget screen on a five-year-old phone, that's okay — you can compete on quality, speed, warranty, and service rather than price.

Underpricing is easy to fall into and genuinely hard to fix later. Raising prices on existing customers is awkward. It's much easier to start at the right price and stay there.

2. Not taking deposits

Every repair shop has had the experience of ordering a part for a specific job, paying for it, and then having the customer disappear. They don't answer calls. They don't come back. You've now got a part you bought for a specific device that you may never be able to use.

Deposits solve this. A standard approach is to take a deposit equal to the cost of the part — so if the screen costs you £40, you take £40 upfront. The customer is now financially committed. If they don't come back, you've at least covered your parts cost.

New owners often skip this because it feels awkward to ask for money before doing any work. It isn't. It's standard in any service business that involves ordering specific materials. A mechanic takes a deposit. A tailor takes a deposit. You should too.

3. Skipping a proper intake process

Verbal agreements feel fine in the moment. The customer describes the fault, you name a price, they leave the phone. Simple. The problem is that verbal agreements are your word against theirs when anything goes wrong.

A proper intake process captures the key information in writing: the device, the fault, the agreed price, any pre-existing damage, and a signature or at least a written acknowledgement. This isn't about being bureaucratic — it's about having a record that protects both you and the customer.

The most costly version of skipping this is the pre-existing damage problem. A customer drops off a phone with a hairline crack in the corner. You don't note it. You do the screen repair. The customer comes back and points at the crack, saying you did it during the repair. Without a note at intake, you have no record proving otherwise.

A simple intake form — even a paper one — makes this problem go away entirely.

4. Buying too much inventory upfront

Before they really know their demand, a lot of new repair shop owners stock up. They buy screens for the twenty most popular phones, batteries for another ten models, charging ports, cameras. The logic makes sense — having parts on hand means faster turnarounds.

The reality is that demand for specific models is unpredictable until you've been operating for a while. You might buy ten screens for a model that nobody ever brings in, while the model you're constantly getting calls about is the one you didn't stock. Inventory that doesn't move is cash sitting on a shelf.

The better approach when starting out is to order parts per job. Yes, it means slightly slower turnarounds while you wait for parts. But it also means zero unsold stock and no cash tied up unnecessarily. Once you have six months of data on which models you repair most often, you can make much better decisions about what to keep in stock.

5. Ignoring Google Business Profile

Google Business Profile is a free tool that puts your shop on Google Maps, shows your opening hours, lets customers leave reviews, and gives you a presence in local search results for queries like "phone repair near me." It is, without question, the most cost-effective marketing tool available to a small repair shop.

Most new owners set it up once, get it roughly right, and then forget about it. The shops that do well with it actively manage it: they respond to every review (positive and negative), post updates occasionally, keep their hours accurate, and add photos of their shop and work.

If you haven't claimed your listing yet, do it this week. If you have claimed it but haven't looked at it in three months, look at it now. Reviews are the single most important factor in whether a local customer chooses your shop over a competitor, and they're entirely free to generate.

6. No system for tracking jobs

At one or two repairs a week, tracking jobs in your head or in a notebook is manageable. At ten a week, it starts to get messy. At twenty or more, it breaks down entirely — and the failure modes are genuinely costly: lost devices, disputed repairs, customers waiting longer than necessary because nobody noticed a job was ready.

Paper is fine to start. A simple carbon-copy job ticket book with a numbered system gets you a long way. But digital is significantly better once you're past a handful of repairs a day. The ability to search for any job instantly, see all open jobs at a glance, and track the status of everything without physically checking is worth a lot when you're busy.

The mistake isn't using paper — it's having no system at all. Even the most basic system that creates a record for every job and tracks whether it's done is better than nothing.

7. Doing everything yourself too long

Being the only person in the shop means you can't take holidays, can't be sick, and can't focus on growing the business because you're too busy running it. A lot of repair shop owners hit a ceiling at exactly the level of work one person can do, and stay there for years because they never build the systems or the team to go beyond it.

The fix isn't necessarily hiring someone straightaway — staff have real costs and complications. It's about building systems that would allow someone else to help if needed. Standard operating procedures for intake. Clear pricing that doesn't require your personal judgement on every job. A ticket system that anyone can use. These things make delegation possible, and delegation is what creates headroom for growth.

The signal that it's time to think about this is usually when you're turning away work because you're too busy, or when you're working hours that aren't sustainable. At that point, the question isn't whether to change something — it's what to change first.
