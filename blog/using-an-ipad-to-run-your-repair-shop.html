Using an iPad to Run Your Repair Shop

4 Feb 2026 5 min read
← All articles

Walk into a modern repair shop and you're increasingly likely to see an iPad propped on the counter instead of a chunky Windows till or a cluttered laptop. It's not just aesthetics — there are real practical reasons why iPad has become the go-to hardware for independent repair shops, and those reasons compound the longer you run your business on one.

This guide covers everything you need to know to run a repair shop from an iPad: why it makes sense, what hardware to pair it with, and how to make it work as your full operational hub — not just a fancy notepad.

Why iPad beats a traditional till for repair shops

Portability

A traditional electronic point-of-sale till is bolted to the counter. An iPad goes wherever you need it. You can carry it to the bench while you diagnose a device, hand it to a customer to review their quote, or take it to a repair fair or market stall. That flexibility matters in a job where you're not always standing behind a counter.

Touch interface

Tap to update a job status. Tap to mark a payment. Swipe to move between tickets. The touch interface on iPad is faster for most POS tasks than a keyboard and mouse — especially when your hands are occupied and you need to log something quickly.

App Store

A traditional till runs proprietary software that can't be updated without vendor involvement. An iPad runs whatever apps best suit your business, updated regularly by developers who are competing for your attention. The quality of software available for iPad — including purpose-built repair shop management tools — is vastly higher than what's built into legacy till systems.

Price

An iPad 10th generation starts at around £329. A decent traditional EPOS till system typically starts at £400–600, and often requires ongoing software subscriptions on top. For a solo repairer, iPad is the cheaper, more capable option from day one.

What apps are useful

Your iPad setup doesn't need to be complicated. For most repair shops, you need four categories of software:

  • Repair POS: The core of your operation — job intake, ticket management, status tracking, payment records. PhoneRepairPOS is built specifically for this.
  • Card payments: Stripe Reader or Square Reader paired with their respective apps lets you take card payments directly from the iPad, with the transaction automatically linked to your sale.
  • Notes / camera: The built-in Notes app and camera are underrated tools. Photograph the condition of a device at intake, drop it into the job note, and you have a timestamped record of any pre-existing damage.
  • Calendar / reminders: For tracking promised turnaround times and following up with customers who haven't collected. The built-in Calendar app handles this fine.
Keep it simple

Resist the urge to install ten different apps. One good repair management app replaces most of what you'd otherwise cobble together from notes, spreadsheets, and calendar entries.

Hardware setup

iPad stand

A good counter stand transforms the iPad from a tablet you keep picking up to a fixed workstation. Look for one with a swivel base so you can rotate it towards the customer when they're reviewing a quote or signing for a repair. POS-style stands with a lockable bracket are worth the extra cost if you're leaving the iPad unattended on the counter.

Recommended options: Heckler Design WindFall (premium, sturdy), Maclocks iPad counter stand (mid-range), or any adjustable arm stand for a more flexible setup on the bench.

Cash drawer (optional)

If you take cash regularly, a USB or Bluetooth cash drawer connects to the iPad and can be triggered to open from within a POS app when a cash payment is recorded. It's not essential — many solo repairers just use a manual till drawer and record cash payments in the app separately — but it tightens up the workflow if cash volume justifies it.

Receipt printer (optional)

Thermal receipt printers that connect via Bluetooth (such as the Star Micronics mPOP or TSP100) can print receipts directly from compatible iPad apps. Most customers are happy with an emailed or SMS receipt, so this is genuinely optional. Worth considering if your clientele skews older or if you're in a market where paper receipts are expected.

Connecting a card reader

The two most popular options for small repair shops are Stripe Reader and Square Reader. Both pair with the iPad via Bluetooth and connect to their respective apps.

Stripe Terminal: Best if you want to build a tighter integration with custom software, or if you already use Stripe for online payments. Transaction fees are 1.5% + 20p for UK cards.

Square Reader: Easier to set up, with a more polished out-of-the-box experience. The Square POS app is capable on its own, though it's not repair-specific. Transaction fees are 1.75% per tap/chip payment.

Both are contactless, support chip and PIN, and let you refund directly from the app. For a solo repairer, either works well — the choice often comes down to which feels simpler to set up.

You don't need to use your card reader's native POS app to process payments. Record the job in your repair management app, take payment on the card reader, and mark the job as paid. Two apps, thirty seconds.

Real-world use: bench vs counter

The iPad setup works slightly differently depending on whether you're at the counter or at the bench.

At the counter: The iPad sits on its stand, facing you. A customer brings in a device. You open a new job, fill in the intake details, quote a price, and hand the iPad across for the customer to confirm. You take a deposit if required. The ticket is live and visible from anywhere in the shop.

At the bench: You pick up the iPad, navigate to the open ticket for the device you're working on, and log notes as you go — what you found, what parts you're using, any complications. If you need to update the status or leave a note for later, it takes ten seconds and you put the iPad back down.

The portability is what makes this work. A till bolted to the counter can't travel to the bench. An iPad can be wherever the work is.
