Using an iPad to Run Your Repair Shop

14 Mar 2026 6 min read
← All articles

An iPad sitting on a counter does not look like a full repair shop management system. But it can be one. Customer intake, repair tickets, job status tracking, payment processing, customer history — all of it runs on the same device that people carry around their kitchen to watch recipe videos.

That familiarity is actually one of the strongest arguments for using an iPad as your shop's primary tool. You do not need training to pick one up and start tapping. Your staff already know the interface. Customers recognise it as a professional, modern piece of equipment. And the ecosystem of accessories and apps built around iPad makes it remarkably capable for a business of any size.

This guide covers the practical setup: what hardware you need, what software to choose, what an iPad can realistically replace in your shop, and how to configure it for a solo repairer versus a small team.

The obvious point, but worth saying

You already know how to use an iPad. That is the point. The learning curve for your staff is close to zero, which means less time training and more time repairing.

Why an iPad makes sense for a repair shop counter

The repair shop counter is a specific environment. Space is usually limited. You are constantly moving — from the counter to the workbench, from the workbench to the stock room. Customers stand directly in front of you rather than across a desk. You need a device that can turn to face a customer for signature, then turn back for your own use, then be carried to the bench without a second thought.

A desktop computer fails on portability. A laptop fails on counter presence and touchscreen interaction. A phone screen is too small for intake forms and job lists. An iPad sits cleanly in the middle: big enough to be useful, light enough to move freely, and touch-native so every interaction feels natural.

The hardware is also reliable. iPads from the last four years — including the base iPad 10th generation and the iPad mini — are fast, have good batteries, and are backed by Apple's ecosystem of accessories. They hold their value reasonably well and are straightforward to replace if something goes wrong.

What hardware you need

The core setup is minimal:

  • An iPad. Any iPad from the last four years is capable. You do not need a Pro. The standard iPad (9th or 10th generation) or iPad mini is more than sufficient for repair shop software. A larger screen — iPad Air or iPad Pro — is nice for viewing job lists but not necessary.
  • A stand or case. For counter use, a weighted stand that holds the iPad at an angle is ideal. For a shop where you also carry the iPad to the bench, a rugged case with a kickstand works well. The key is stability — you do not want a customer's intake session interrupted because the iPad tips over.
  • A card reader. Square, SumUp, and Stripe Terminal all produce Bluetooth card readers that pair with an iPad. Each has different fee structures and slightly different hardware options. Choose one that suits your transaction volume and preferred integration with your shop software.
  • A receipt printer (optional). Many customers are happy with a digital receipt via email or SMS. If you prefer to print, Bluetooth receipt printers from Star Micronics or Epson are widely used and pair with iPad without needing a separate driver installation.

That is the complete setup for a solo repairer. Total hardware cost is typically in the range of £300–£600 depending on which iPad model you choose and which card reader you go with. Compare that with the cost of a traditional till system, receipt printer, barcode scanner, and PC, and the iPad setup is significantly cheaper — and occupies a fraction of the counter space.

What iPad software can replace in your shop

A purpose-built iPad repair shop app like PhoneRepairPOS is designed to handle the core operational workflow end to end:

  • Paper intake tickets. Customer name, contact details, device make and model, fault description, agreed quote, and any pre-existing damage notes can all be logged digitally at intake. No more illegible handwriting, lost tickets, or mystery devices with no record.
  • The traditional till. With a card reader paired to your iPad, you can take card payments directly through your repair app or through a payment app. Cash can be managed through the same interface if your software supports it.
  • A PC-based POS. If you were previously running a point-of-sale system on a Windows computer, an iPad running native repair software is a direct replacement — faster to set up, easier to use, and less prone to the kind of slow-start mornings that come with an aging PC.
  • A physical job board. Some shops use whiteboards or sticky notes to track which repairs are waiting for parts, in progress, or ready to collect. A digital job list on the iPad replaces this more reliably, because it can be updated from anywhere and does not get accidentally wiped.

What iPad does not replace

Being honest about the limits is useful. An iPad running repair shop software is not a complete accounting system. You will still need separate accounting software — Xero, QuickBooks, or FreeAgent are popular for small UK businesses — for VAT returns, expense tracking, and end-of-year reporting. Your repair software can export transaction data to feed into these systems, but it is not a substitute for them.

At scale, inventory management also has limits. For a solo repairer or a shop with a handful of technicians, tracking parts through your repair tickets and a simple spreadsheet is often sufficient. But a multi-location operation with dozens of SKUs and multiple suppliers will eventually need dedicated inventory software. For most single-location repair shops, that point is further away than people expect.

Recommended setup for a solo repairer

If you are running the shop by yourself, simplicity is the priority. You do not need complex user accounts or shift logging. You need intake to be fast, job status to be visible at a glance, and payment to be frictionless at collection.

Practically, this means: iPad on a stand on the counter, card reader paired and ready, repair app open full screen. At intake, you create a new job ticket, add the customer details, log the fault and agreed price, and hand the screen to the customer to confirm. The ticket is live immediately. When the repair is done, you update the status, the customer collects, and you take payment through the same screen.

The whole workflow happens on one device without switching apps. That is the operational efficiency a well-built iPad setup delivers.

Recommended setup for a small team

With two or three technicians, you can run a shared iPad at the counter for intake and collection, with each technician updating job status as they work. Because PhoneRepairPOS stores data locally, there is no login delay or sync lag — updates are instant.

If you want technicians to update jobs from the bench without coming to the counter, a second iPad on the bench running the same app and syncing over your local network is straightforward to set up. This is particularly useful when you have repairs at different stages simultaneously and want your counter iPad to always show accurate current status without the bench technician having to walk over to update it.

Common concerns addressed

"What if the screen gets cracked?" Use a rugged case. Options from OtterBox, UAG, and similar manufacturers are specifically designed for commercial environments and will protect an iPad from the kind of accidental drops that happen on a workbench. A cracked iPad screen is also cheap to repair, which is useful given your line of work.

"What if the iPad itself breaks or is stolen?" This is where choosing software that stores data in the cloud (in addition to locally) matters. PhoneRepairPOS syncs data when connected, which means your entire job history, customer records, and pricing data can be restored to a new device quickly. You are never more than a few taps from being operational again.

"Won't customers think it is less professional than a proper till?" The opposite tends to be true. Customers — particularly younger ones — associate an iPad-based setup with modern, competent businesses. A clunky Windows POS or a handwritten paper ticket is what signals that a shop is behind the times.

Getting started

If you are starting from scratch, the fastest path to a working iPad setup is: buy a mid-range iPad, download PhoneRepairPOS, pair a Square or SumUp card reader, and set up your most common repair types and prices. You can be taking jobs within an hour of unboxing.

If you are transitioning from an existing system — paper tickets, a different app, or a PC-based POS — the main task is migrating your customer list and pricing structure. Most good repair apps make this straightforward with import tools or simple manual setup.

The iPad is not a compromise solution for small shops that cannot afford proper equipment. It is the right tool for this environment. Light, portable, familiar, connected, and increasingly capable as the app ecosystem around it grows.
