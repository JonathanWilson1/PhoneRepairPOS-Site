iPad POS vs Traditional Till

6 Mar 2026 6 min read
← All articles

If you're setting up a phone repair shop — or thinking about replacing an ageing till — you've probably landed on the same question: do you buy a traditional cash register, or go with an iPad POS system?

It's a more important decision than it looks. Your point of sale isn't just where money changes hands. For a repair shop, it's also where you log jobs, track status, store customer details, and handle collections. The wrong choice doesn't just cost money — it creates friction every single day.

This guide walks through every angle that matters for a small repair business. No generic retail advice. Just the stuff that's relevant when you're running a shop where the product you're selling is a service, not a physical item off a shelf.

Upfront Cost

Traditional electronic tills range from around £150 for a basic model to £600+ for something with a proper receipt printer and cash drawer built in. Add a separate card reader and you're pushing £800 before you've logged a single job.

An iPad setup starts with the iPad itself — a refurbished iPad (9th gen or Air) runs £250–£400 new, or less if you buy second-hand. You'll want a card reader (Stripe or Square terminals start around £49–£79), and optionally a Bluetooth receipt printer (£80–£150). All in, you can have a solid iPad POS setup for £350–£600, with the iPad being an asset you'll use for other things too.

On upfront cost alone, iPad wins — especially when you factor in that the iPad has a resale value and can be repurposed if you change systems.

Ongoing Costs

Traditional tills have near-zero ongoing costs. You buy it, you use it, you maintain it. Some manufacturers charge for software updates, but most don't. The till just sits there and works.

iPad POS software typically charges a monthly fee. This varies significantly — some apps charge £20–£30/month, others are free with a transaction fee, and a few charge a one-time purchase price with no recurring costs. Before you commit, calculate what 12 months of software costs actually adds up to. A £25/month app costs £300/year, which eats into your upfront savings quickly.

That said, the software cost usually comes with cloud backup, updates, and new features included — things a static till simply doesn't offer.

Flexibility and Portability

A traditional till is bolted to the counter. It works exactly as it did the day you bought it, and that's it. If you want to work at a pop-up market, a repair event, or even just meet a customer to collect their device, it can't come with you.

An iPad goes wherever you go. You can take payments at the counter, in the back of the shop, at a customer's door, or at an event. For a sole trader who sometimes does mobile repairs, this is a genuine advantage.

Even in a fixed shop setting, the flexibility matters. You can update your price list, add a new repair type, or change a labour rate from the iPad in seconds — without needing a technician to reprogram a till.

Repair-Specific Features

This is where the comparison becomes most relevant for your business — and where a traditional till loses badly.

A standard till processes sales. That's it. It doesn't know that a customer left their iPhone 13 with you on Monday, that you're waiting on a screen from your supplier, or that the device is ready for collection. You have to manage all of that separately — with a paper book, a spreadsheet, a whiteboard, or whatever system you've cobbled together.

A repair-specific iPad POS — like PhoneRepairPOS — handles the full job lifecycle:

  • Job intake: log the device, fault description, customer details, and estimated price when the customer drops off
  • Status tracking: move jobs from "Awaiting Parts" to "In Progress" to "Ready for Collection" with a tap
  • Customer records: see a customer's repair history instantly when they walk in again
  • Job tickets: print or send a receipt at drop-off so customers have a reference number
  • Payment at collection: take payment and close the job in the same flow

None of this is possible with a traditional till. You'd need a separate job management system, which adds cost and complexity. With an iPad POS designed for repair shops, it's all in one place.

Ease of Use

Modern iPad apps are designed to be intuitive. If your staff can use a smartphone, they can learn an iPad POS in under an hour. Updates happen silently in the background. The interface doesn't change dramatically between versions.

Traditional tills can be surprisingly fiddly — especially older ones. Programming product codes, changing prices, or adding new categories often requires consulting a manual or calling a supplier. New staff members sometimes find them confusing because the button layout isn't always logical.

For a shop where you might have part-time staff or cover during holidays, ease of onboarding matters more than you'd think.

Reliability and What Happens When Things Go Wrong

A traditional till runs on its own hardware and software. If the hardware fails, you buy a new till. There's no cloud, no syncing, no internet dependency. In that sense, they're extremely reliable in a narrow way.

An iPad POS relies on the iPad (which is durable consumer hardware), the app (which can be updated or discontinued), and optionally the cloud (which requires internet). Good iPad POS apps work fully offline — so if your broadband drops, you can still take payments and log jobs. Data syncs when you're back online.

The risk with any software-based system is that the company behind it could change pricing or discontinue the product. This is worth considering when choosing a provider.

When a traditional till still makes sense

If you genuinely only need to process sales — no job tracking, no customer records, no status updates — and you don't want any monthly software costs or internet dependency, a traditional electronic till is simple and cheap to run. It makes most sense for shops that handle very high-volume, low-complexity transactions, like a market stall or a small accessories kiosk. For most phone repair shops, though, the job management gap is a dealbreaker.

Switching Costs

Switching from a traditional till to an iPad POS is relatively straightforward. You're adding new software, not replacing deeply embedded infrastructure. Your existing customer data probably lives in a notebook or spreadsheet, so migrating it is a manual job either way.

Switching the other direction — from an iPad POS back to a till — would mean losing all your repair history, customer records, and reporting. It's technically possible but you'd be going backwards in capability. Most shop owners who try an iPad POS system don't go back.

What Kind of Shop Suits Each

A traditional till suits shops that: have very simple operations (accessories retail only, no repair service), are highly cost-sensitive and want zero recurring costs, or have a strong preference for hardware that isn't dependent on software.

An iPad POS suits shops that: do repair work and need job/ticket management, want customer history and reporting, have staff who need an easy-to-learn system, want the flexibility to work mobile or at multiple locations, or want a system that can grow with the business.

For the vast majority of phone repair shops — which means a shop where repair is the core service, not just an add-on — an iPad POS built specifically for repair work is the right choice. The upfront cost is comparable, the ongoing cost is justified by the features, and the day-to-day difference in running the shop is significant.
