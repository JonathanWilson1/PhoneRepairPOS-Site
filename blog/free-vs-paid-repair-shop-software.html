Free vs Paid Repair Shop Software: What You Actually Get

11 Mar 2026 6 min read
← All articles

When you're starting a phone repair shop, keeping costs down makes sense. So it's natural to look at free software first. A quick search turns up spreadsheet templates, generic free POS apps, and Google Sheets-based systems — all promising to handle your repair workflow at zero cost.

This article gives you an honest look at what free tools actually provide, what they quietly cost you, and when it genuinely makes sense to pay for something built specifically for repair shops.

What free repair shop software actually includes

Let's be clear about what "free" usually means in this context. You're typically looking at one of three things:

Spreadsheets and Google Sheets. You build your own system. A row per repair, columns for device, customer name, fault description, status. It works at very low volume. Some repair shops share templates online, and these can get you surprisingly far if you're diligent about keeping them updated.

Generic free POS apps. These are designed for retail or cafes, not repair shops. You can usually log a sale and take payment, but there's no concept of a job ticket, no status tracking, no place to store diagnostic notes, and no way to tell a customer their phone is ready for collection without checking manually.

Freemium repair software. Some repair-specific tools offer a free tier. These can be genuinely useful, but the free tier is usually heavily limited — one user, a cap on monthly tickets, no customer history beyond a few weeks, or branding you can't remove. The features that actually save you time are often locked behind a paid plan.

What free gives you

The obvious answer: zero monthly cost. If you're doing one or two repairs a week, have no staff, and are still figuring out whether this is going to work as a business, free tools are completely reasonable. The financial risk of paying for software before you have consistent volume is real, and there's no shame in using a spreadsheet while you find your feet.

Free also gives you flexibility. You can build a Google Sheet exactly how you want it. There's no learning curve beyond your own setup. And if you stop for a month, nothing expires.

What free costs you

This is the part that doesn't show up in the price comparison. Free software costs you time — and in a repair shop, time is money.

Here's what manual, free-tool workflows typically require:

  • Manually updating a spreadsheet every time a job moves forward
  • Texting or calling customers individually to say their phone is ready
  • Digging through a list of rows to find a specific job when a customer calls
  • Tracking unpaid jobs with no alerts or overdue indicators
  • No record of a customer's previous repairs, so every interaction starts from scratch
  • No way to see at a glance which jobs are waiting for parts, which are in progress, and which are done

Each of these is a small friction point on its own. But across twenty or thirty jobs a week, they add up to a significant chunk of your working day spent on admin rather than on repairs.

There's also a reliability problem. Spreadsheets don't send you reminders. They don't flag when a job has been sitting uncollected for two weeks. They don't keep a timestamped record of who changed what. When something goes wrong — a disputed repair, a missing device, a customer who swears they paid — the paper or spreadsheet trail is rarely good enough to protect you.

The real cost of free software is your time

Free tools don't have a monthly fee. But they cost you time on admin every single day. At even £15/hour, 30 minutes of daily admin overhead adds up to over £2,700 a year — significantly more than most paid repair shop software costs.

When free is fine

There are situations where free tools genuinely make sense, and there's no point pretending otherwise.

You're just starting out. If you're doing repairs from home, taking in jobs from friends and family, and still figuring out your pricing and process, a spreadsheet is fine. The overhead of learning a new tool is real, and the volume doesn't justify it yet.

You're doing fewer than five repairs a day. At low volume, the manual work is manageable. You probably know all your active jobs from memory anyway. The inefficiencies exist, but they're not yet painful enough to be a real problem.

You're a one-person operation with flexible time. If you have no staff and a quiet enough day that you can spend time on admin without it affecting your repairs, free tools won't hurt you much.

When it makes sense to pay

The calculation changes once you hit a certain level of activity. The specific threshold varies by shop, but there are clear signals that free tools are holding you back.

You're doing 5+ repairs a day. At this volume, manual status updates and customer notifications become a genuine burden. The time you spend on admin is time you're not spending on repairs — which means real revenue loss.

You have more than one person working. Spreadsheets are not built for collaboration. Two people updating the same Google Sheet simultaneously creates confusion, missed updates, and version conflicts. A proper system with real-time sync eliminates this entirely.

You want payment records and customer history. Knowing that a customer has been in three times, what devices they've brought, and whether they still owe you money is genuinely useful. It helps you recognise regulars, resolve disputes, and understand your business. Free tools either don't track this or make it very difficult to access.

You've had a dispute or lost a job. If you've ever had a customer claim they paid when they didn't, or a device go missing because a paper ticket fell behind the counter, the cost of a missed record has already made itself clear. A digital system that timestamps every action removes most of the ambiguity from these situations.

How much paid software actually costs

Repair-specific software typically falls in the £10–£50 per month range, depending on the number of users and the features included. At the lower end, you're usually getting one user with core job tracking and customer records. At the higher end, multi-user access, inventory management, reporting, and integrations.

Some tools charge per-ticket fees on top of a base subscription, which can add up if you have high volume. It's worth checking the pricing structure carefully before committing.

iPad-native apps like PhoneRepairPOS tend to be at the more accessible end of the pricing range — built specifically for small repair shop owners who don't need enterprise features, just something that works cleanly on the counter.

The ROI framing that actually matters

The question isn't really "can I afford to pay for software?" It's "what does my time cost, and does this software save more than it costs?"

If paid software saves you 30 minutes of admin per day — a conservative estimate once you're past ten jobs a day — and you value your time at £15 an hour, that's £7.50 per day, or roughly £165 per month. Software that costs £20/month and saves you 30 minutes daily pays for itself eight times over.

The savings are usually larger than that once you account for reduced disputes, faster customer communication, and fewer jobs that fall through the cracks. But even the conservative version makes the maths fairly clear.

Free software is a reasonable starting point. It's not a permanent solution once your shop is busy. The switch from free to paid is one of the most straightforward business decisions you'll make — and the right time to make it is before the free tool becomes genuinely painful, not after.
