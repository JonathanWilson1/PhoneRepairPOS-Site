How to Price Screen Repairs for Profit

13 Mar 2026 6 min read
← All articles

Screen replacements are the core of most phone repair businesses. They're high volume, repeatable, and the work is learnable. They're also where a lot of shops quietly leave money on the table — either by underpricing to compete, using parts without understanding the cost differences, or failing to account for the time the job actually takes.

This guide focuses specifically on screen repair pricing: how to build a price that covers your costs, pays you properly for your time, and holds up against competitive pressure without racing to the bottom.

OEM, aftermarket, and refurbished: understanding your parts options

The screen you use has a huge impact on both your cost and what you can legitimately charge. There are three main categories:

OEM (Original Equipment Manufacturer) screens are either genuine parts pulled from new or refurb units, or parts made by the same supplier that made the original components. They match the original quality exactly and typically come with full Touch ID, Face ID, and True Tone compatibility (depending on the model). They're also significantly more expensive — on a current-model iPhone, an OEM screen can cost two to three times as much as a budget aftermarket alternative.

Aftermarket screens vary enormously in quality. Budget aftermarket screens are cheap but often noticeably inferior — reduced brightness, worse colour accuracy, different feel under the finger. Mid-range aftermarket screens are much better, often indistinguishable to most users. Understanding exactly what you're buying matters here: a screen listed as "aftermarket" could be anywhere on this spectrum.

Refurbished original screens are genuine Apple or Samsung screens that have been cleaned and reglassed. They maintain original display quality but can be more expensive than standard aftermarket alternatives, and their supply is dependent on used parts availability.

The pricing implication is straightforward: the part you use determines what you can charge, and you need to communicate the difference clearly to customers. A shop that only offers one screen option at one price is leaving both money and customer satisfaction on the table.

Labour time per model: why this matters more than you think

Screen repair time varies significantly by device. An older Samsung Galaxy might take 20–25 minutes for a competent technician. An iPhone 14 Pro with a complex adhesive and multiple components to disconnect can take 60–90 minutes. If you're charging the same flat rate for both, one of those jobs is profitable and the other may not be.

The practical solution is model-specific pricing, at least for your most common repairs. Your price list shouldn't just be "screen repair: £X" — it should be "iPhone 14 Pro screen: £X, Samsung S24 screen: £X" and so on. This lets you reflect the actual time and complexity involved rather than averaging everything to a number that works for some jobs and doesn't for others.

A rough way to think about it: for each additional 30 minutes of labour versus your quickest screen replacement, add the equivalent of 30 minutes of your target hourly rate to the price. If you value your time at £40/hour, that's an extra £20. On a £120 repair, that's meaningful.

Overhead allocation: the costs that don't show up on the invoice

Parts cost and labour time are visible. Overhead is the invisible cost that erodes margin when you ignore it.

Your overhead includes rent (or the notional cost of your location), utilities, tools and equipment, insurance, software, consumables like adhesive and cleaning solutions, and the cost of any warranty claims or redos. These costs exist whether or not you're doing a repair, and they need to be covered by your revenue.

A simple approach is to calculate your total monthly overhead and divide it by the number of jobs you do per month. If your overhead is £1,500/month and you do 100 jobs, each job needs to contribute £15 towards overhead before it turns a profit. That £15 needs to be in your pricing, on top of parts cost and labour.

How to handle competition from cheap online quotes

Every repair shop deals with customers who've found a quote online for significantly less than you charge. Sometimes it's a legitimate competitor with lower costs. Often, it's a quote for a budget aftermarket screen with no warranty, sent by a shop that will upsell aggressively once the phone is in their hands.

The honest response is to explain exactly what you're offering: which type of screen, what warranty, what happens if there's a problem, and how long you've been operating. Customers who care only about price will go with the cheapest option regardless. Customers who care about quality and reliability can make a better decision when they understand what they're comparing.

Don't drop your price to match a quote you can't verify. You don't know what parts or process that quote assumes, and you can't profitably do high-quality work at budget prices. Compete on what you can actually deliver, not on price alone.

Pricing psychology: small choices that affect perception

How you present prices matters. A few practical observations:

Odd pricing works. £79 feels meaningfully cheaper than £80 to most customers, even though the difference is negligible. This is well-documented in retail and applies equally to repair shops.

Anchoring with a premium option changes what feels reasonable. If you offer three screen options — economy at £59, standard at £89, premium OEM at £129 — the standard option feels like the obvious middle ground. If you only offer one option at £89, some customers will feel it's expensive without a reference point. Offering tiers doesn't just serve different customers; it changes how each tier is perceived.

Transparency builds trust. A shop that explains what each tier includes — and why the premium option costs more — will convert more customers on higher-margin repairs than one that just quotes a number. Most people don't actually need the cheapest option; they just need to feel confident the price is fair.

Example pricing model: three tiers for iPhone screen repair
  • Economy (£59–£69): Budget aftermarket screen. Functional quality. Standard 30-day warranty. Suitable for older devices or customers on a tight budget.
  • Standard (£89–£109): High-quality aftermarket or manufacturer-grade screen. Near-original display quality. 90-day warranty. Recommended for most customers.
  • Premium OEM (£129–£159): Genuine original screen. Original brightness, colour accuracy, and feature compatibility. 6-month warranty. Recommended for new devices and customers who want original quality.

Deposit policy on screen repairs

Screen repairs on current-model iPhones and Samsungs involve expensive parts. An OEM iPhone 15 Pro screen costs upwards of £100 before labour. Ordering this without a deposit means carrying that cost if the customer doesn't collect.

A reasonable policy is to take a deposit equal to the parts cost at time of booking. This covers your exposure if the customer doesn't come back, and it gives customers who are genuinely committed no reason to object. Customers who won't pay a deposit often aren't committed to the repair — better to find that out before you order the part.

When to decline a repair

Not every screen repair is worth doing. There are situations where taking the job creates more risk than revenue:

  • Cracked or bent frame. A screen fitted to a damaged frame is unlikely to sit correctly and is prone to cracking again. The customer will often blame the screen rather than the frame, leading to a dispute.
  • Prior water damage. A phone with a water-damaged board may fail days after a screen repair for unrelated reasons. The customer will assume the repair caused it.
  • Very old device. If the repair cost approaches or exceeds the device's value, many customers will be dissatisfied even with a technically successful repair. A phone worth £50 that needs an £80 screen repair is rarely worth doing — and worth telling the customer that upfront.

Declining a repair honestly is always better than doing it badly or setting up a future dispute. Customers who appreciate that honesty are worth more in the long run than the one-time revenue from a job that goes wrong.
