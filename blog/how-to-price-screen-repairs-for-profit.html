How to Price Screen Repairs for Profit

20 Feb 2026 6 min read
← All articles

Screen replacements are the bread and butter of most phone repair shops — high volume, relatively quick to complete, and in constant demand. But pricing them badly is also one of the fastest ways to build a busy business that doesn't actually make money. Here's how to price screen repairs properly.

Start with the real cost

Before you can price for profit, you need to know what a repair actually costs you. Most shops only count the part. The full cost is:

  • Part cost — what you paid for the screen, including any shipping
  • Labour time — the actual minutes spent on the repair, valued at a realistic hourly rate
  • Overhead allocation — a share of your rent, insurance, tools, and software costs spread across each repair

Let's work through a real example. Suppose you're replacing the screen on an iPhone 14. An aftermarket screen costs around £25. The repair takes 30 minutes. If you value your time at £20/hour, that's £10 in labour. If your monthly overheads are £500 and you do 80 repairs a month, each repair carries a £6.25 overhead contribution. Total real cost: approximately £41.25.

That's your floor. Any price below that is a loss, even if cash is hitting the till.

OEM vs aftermarket parts

The parts market for phone screens splits broadly into three tiers:

Aftermarket / compatible screens are the cheapest option. Quality varies significantly between suppliers. Budget aftermarket screens (£15–£25 for an iPhone 14) often have lower brightness, less accurate colour, and shorter lifespans. Better-grade aftermarket screens cost more but perform closer to the original.

OEM-equivalent / service pack parts come from the same manufacturer supply chain as the original components, or are genuine parts pulled from other devices. These are significantly better quality but cost considerably more — often 2–3x the price of basic aftermarket.

Apple genuine parts are available through Apple's Independent Repair Provider programme. They're the highest quality but come with constraints on how they can be used and require calibration tools.

Your pricing needs to reflect which tier of parts you're using. Charging premium prices with budget parts leads to callbacks. Charging aftermarket prices for OEM-quality parts leaves money on the table.

Offer a choice

Many shops offer two tiers: standard (aftermarket) and premium (OEM/service pack). This lets price-sensitive customers self-select the cheaper option without you having to discount across the board.

Setting a target margin

A healthy gross margin for screen repairs is typically 40–60%. That means if you charge £100, your combined parts and labour cost should be between £40 and £60.

To calculate your price from a target margin: divide your cost by (1 minus your target margin). If your cost is £41 and you want a 55% margin: £41 ÷ 0.45 = £91. Round to £90 or £95 depending on local competition.

If you're currently operating at margins below 40%, you're either underpricing or overpaying for parts. Both are fixable.

Benchmarking competitors — without racing to the bottom

Check what two or three established shops in your area charge for the same repairs. Search Google for "iPhone screen repair [your town]" and look at who's appearing on the map and what their listed prices are.

The goal isn't to match the cheapest. The goal is to understand the range and position yourself sensibly within it. If the cheapest shop charges £60 and the most established charges £95, there's nothing wrong with charging £85 if you have good reviews and a professional presentation.

Customers aren't only buying a screen repair. They're buying confidence that their phone will come back in one piece, on time, with someone they can call if something goes wrong.

Competing purely on price means you'll always be chasing the next shop willing to go lower. Competing on reliability, trust, and reviews means you can sustain higher prices over time.

Adjusting for model difficulty

Not all screen repairs take the same time. An iPhone SE takes 15–20 minutes. An iPhone 15 Pro Max with Face ID calibration takes considerably longer. Samsung foldables are in another category entirely.

Your pricing should reflect this. A flat "screen replacement £65" price list is simple but means you're effectively subsidising difficult repairs with easy ones. A tiered price list — grouping models by complexity — is more accurate and more profitable.

Practical groupings:

  • Tier 1 (simple): older iPhones (SE, 8), basic Androids
  • Tier 2 (standard): iPhone 12–14, mid-range Samsung
  • Tier 3 (complex): iPhone 15 Pro/Pro Max, Samsung S series, Plus/Ultra models

Each tier carries a price premium. This is standard across the industry and customers expect it — a Pro Max repair costing more than an SE repair makes intuitive sense.

Volume and positioning: two paths

There are two viable business models for screen repairs, and they require different pricing strategies.

High volume / competitive pricing: You're efficient, your turnaround is fast, and you rely on throughput. You price at or slightly below mid-market and make your margin from the volume of repairs you complete. This model works better in high-footfall locations with lower costs per repair.

Premium positioning: You differentiate on quality, warranty, and professionalism. You charge in the upper range of your market and attract customers who value reliability over price. You do fewer repairs but at higher margins. This model suits shops with strong reviews, a professional setup, and customers who aren't primarily motivated by finding the cheapest option.

Neither is wrong. But trying to do both — charging premium prices while also competing on being the cheapest — doesn't work. Pick a lane and price accordingly.

Review prices regularly

Parts prices change. Postage costs change. Your rent might increase. Prices that made sense eighteen months ago might no longer deliver the margin you need. Set a calendar reminder to review your price list every six months. It's easier to make small adjustments regularly than to face a large gap between your costs and your charges that's been quietly compounding.
