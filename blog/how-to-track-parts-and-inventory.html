How to Track Parts and Inventory as a Solo Repairer

1 Feb 2026 5 min read
← All articles

Ask most solo repairers how they manage inventory and you'll get some version of the same answer: "I keep a rough count in my head, reorder when I think I'm running low, and occasionally get caught out." It's not a system — it's hope. And hope has a habit of letting you down at the worst possible time: when a customer is standing at the counter waiting for a screen replacement you thought you had.

This guide isn't about implementing a full warehouse management system. That's overkill for a one-person shop. It's about a minimum viable approach to parts tracking — one that takes minutes to set up and prevents the most common pain points without adding busywork to your day.

Why solo repairers struggle with inventory

The root cause is usually time, not knowledge. You know you should track stock. You've probably tried before. But when you're busy — and most repairers are nearly always busy — updating a spreadsheet after every repair feels like the last thing worth doing.

There's also an ad hoc buying habit that develops naturally. A customer books in an iPhone 13 screen replacement. You don't have the part. You order one from your usual supplier, do the repair, and move on. That works — until you get three iPhone 13 bookings in the same week and you're waiting on deliveries for two of them.

The other challenge is part fragmentation. Unlike a retailer selling a finite range of products, a repair shop might handle screens for fifty different device models, each with variations by colour and grade. Tracking that manually is genuinely difficult, which is why most people don't bother.

What you actually need to track

You don't need to track everything. Start with the parts you use most often and the ones where running out would cost you the most time and money. For most phone repair shops, that means:

  • Screens by model and colour — your highest-volume, highest-margin repairs. iPhone 14 black, iPhone 14 white, Samsung S23, and so on.
  • Batteries by model — high volume, relatively low cost, but running out creates delays.
  • Charging ports — USB-C and Lightning ports for the models you see most frequently.
  • Adhesive and seals — easy to forget until you're mid-repair. Keep a buffer.
  • Back glass panels — if you offer back glass repairs, these come in model and colour variants too.

Everything else — specialist parts you order on demand for less common repairs — doesn't need to be in your inventory system. Track what you hold stock of; order everything else reactively.

A simple spreadsheet system to start

If you're starting from nothing, a spreadsheet is a perfectly valid first step. You don't need purpose-built software on day one. Here's a minimal structure that works:

  • Column A: Part name (e.g., "iPhone 14 Screen — Black OEM")
  • Column B: Current quantity in stock
  • Column C: Reorder point (the quantity at which you order more)
  • Column D: Preferred supplier
  • Column E: Last ordered

Once a week — Friday afternoon, or whenever you have a quiet ten minutes — update the quantities. It takes less time than you think once the list is set up, because you're just changing numbers rather than building something from scratch.

The key habit

The spreadsheet only works if you update it. The easiest trigger: update the quantity every time you use a part, immediately after the repair. One tap, five seconds. Don't batch it up for later.

Minimum viable reorder system

The reorder point column in your spreadsheet is the most important part. This is the quantity at which you place a new order — not when you run out, but when you get close.

For your most common parts, a reorder point of two is a reasonable starting place. When you're down to two iPhone 14 black screens, you order more. By the time the order arrives (usually one to two days from a UK supplier), you've probably done one or two repairs but still have one in stock as a buffer.

For parts you use less often, a reorder point of one may be fine — order as soon as you open the last one. For adhesive and consumables, reorder when you open the last pack.

The system only works if you check it. A weekly stock check — genuinely just scanning the spreadsheet and comparing numbers to physical count — catches anything that's drifted.

Supplier strategy: bulk vs on demand

This is where inventory management directly affects your margins. Buying parts one at a time from a supplier is convenient but expensive. Buying in bulk reduces unit cost but ties up cash in stock.

The practical approach for a solo repairer:

Buy in bulk for your top five parts. If you're doing fifteen iPhone screen replacements a week, buying screens one at a time is inefficient. Order five or ten at a time. The per-unit saving adds up quickly, and the cash tied up in stock is manageable.

Order on demand for everything else. Parts you use once a month don't need to be stocked. Order them when you need them, keep delivery time in mind when quoting the customer, and don't maintain a buffer for low-frequency repairs.

Use two or three suppliers, not one. Every supplier goes out of stock occasionally. If your single supplier is out of iPhone 15 screens for a week, you're stuck. Having a backup means you're never more than a day away from a part.

Your supplier relationships are part of your inventory system. A supplier who ships next day and communicates stock levels is worth more than one who's marginally cheaper but unreliable.

Avoiding the "I'd fix it but I don't have the part" problem

This is the most expensive inventory failure in a repair shop. A customer brings in a phone, you agree a price, they leave it with you — and then you discover you're out of the part you need. Now you're ringing them back to explain a delay, your reputation takes a small hit, and you've wasted the time you blocked out for the repair.

The fix is a quick stock check at intake, before you confirm the job. When a customer brings in a device for a screen replacement, check your stock before you quote a turnaround time. If you have the part: same day. If you don't: give them a realistic timeline based on when you can get it, and be upfront about it.

Most customers would rather hear "that'll be tomorrow — I need to order the screen" than be told "it'll be ready this afternoon" and get a call at 4pm saying it's not. Managing expectations at intake is free; managing disappointment after a missed promise costs you repeat business.

When to move beyond a spreadsheet

A spreadsheet works well for a small shop with a manageable parts range. It starts to break down when:

  • You have more than one person updating it (version conflicts, overwriting each other's changes)
  • Your parts range grows beyond what's easy to scan visually
  • You want to link parts to specific repair jobs for cost tracking
  • You're opening a second location

At that point, purpose-built repair shop software with integrated parts tracking becomes worthwhile. Start simple, stay consistent

The best inventory system is the one you actually use. A simple spreadsheet updated consistently beats a sophisticated system that gets ignored after the first week. Start with your top ten parts, set reorder points, and build the habit of updating quantities as you work.

Once that's a reflex — and it becomes one faster than you'd expect — you can layer in more detail. Track costs to understand your margins. Link parts to jobs to see which repairs are actually profitable. But none of that matters until the basics are in place.
