How to Track Parts and Inventory as a Solo Repairer

15 Mar 2026 6 min read
← All articles

Search for "repair shop inventory management" and you will find software with barcode scanners, multi-location stock sync, supplier purchase order workflows, and pricing dashboards. Most of it is designed for operations with dozens of SKUs, multiple technicians, and a dedicated person whose job it is to manage stock.

That is not most phone repair shops. Most phone repair shops are run by one or two people who are also the ones doing the actual repairs, taking calls, handling customers at the counter, and ordering parts in the thirty seconds between jobs. A full inventory management system in this context is not helpful — it is another thing to maintain, another thing that can go wrong, and another reason to feel behind.

This article is for the solo or small-team repairer who wants a practical, proportionate approach to tracking parts and stock. One that gives you the information you actually need without requiring a logistics degree to operate.

Start with this principle

The best inventory system for a solo repairer is the one you will actually use. A perfect system you ignore is worth less than a simple one you maintain consistently.

Why most solo repairers do not need a full inventory system

Full inventory management systems are built around a core problem: how do you know what you have, across multiple locations, with multiple people using stock simultaneously, without things falling out of sync?

If you are a single repairer in a single location, most of that complexity does not apply. You know roughly what you have because you bought it, you used it, and you ordered more when you ran low. Your mental model of your stock is reasonably accurate because you are the only person touching it.

The genuine problems you face are simpler: ordering the wrong quantity, not knowing your actual cost on a per-repair basis, getting caught without a critical part mid-job, and mixing up parts without realising which are OEM and which are aftermarket. These are solvable with lightweight systems, not enterprise software.

The right approach early on: order per job

If you are early in your business or repairing a wide variety of device models, the cleanest approach is to order parts per job. A customer books an iPhone 15 screen repair: you order the screen, do the job, and do not carry any buffer stock for that model.

This approach ties up no cash in stock, means you never have parts that go stale or become obsolete, and keeps your storage simple. The trade-off is turnaround time — you cannot promise a same-day repair if you are ordering per job. But for many repair types and many customers, a 24–48 hour turnaround is perfectly acceptable.

Ordering per job also gives you excellent data. After three months, look back at what you have ordered. The parts you ordered most frequently are telling you something: these are your high-volume repairs, and they are candidates for keeping in stock.

Building a buffer stock for your most common repairs

Once you can identify your most common repair types — which usually becomes clear within a few months of trading — it makes sense to keep a small buffer of those parts. Not a warehouse's worth. Just enough to handle a few jobs without waiting for a delivery.

For most solo repairers, this means identifying the top five to ten parts by repair volume. Common candidates include:

  • iPhone screens for the two or three most popular current models
  • Samsung screen assemblies for your most common Samsung repairs
  • Charging ports for the models you fix most frequently
  • Batteries for high-volume battery replacement requests
  • Rear glass panels if you do a lot of back glass repairs

A buffer of two to three units per part is often sufficient for a solo repairer. Enough to handle a run of the same repair type, not so much that you are tying up significant cash in stock that might sit for months.

Restock when you use your last unit, not when you are already out. This is a simple discipline but an important one. The moment you use your last iPhone 14 screen, that is when you order the replacement — not when the next iPhone 14 comes in and you realise you have nothing to fit.

How to track stock: keeping it simple

For a solo repairer with a small buffer stock, a complex system is overkill. A well-maintained spreadsheet — even a note on your phone — is often sufficient. The key fields you need for each part:

  • Part description. Be specific: "iPhone 14 screen — OEM" or "Samsung S23 battery — aftermarket Grade A."
  • Current quantity. How many you have right now.
  • Reorder point. The quantity at which you trigger a new order (usually 1 for low-turnover parts, 2–3 for high-volume).
  • Supplier and part number. So you can reorder in thirty seconds without hunting through emails.
  • Cost price. What you paid per unit. This is important for calculating your margin per repair.

Update the spreadsheet when parts arrive and when you use them. That is it. Two entries per event. If you do this consistently, you will always have an accurate view of your stock without any dedicated software.

Some repair shop apps, including PhoneRepairPOS, allow you to log parts used against each repair ticket. This is useful because it creates an automatic record of what was consumed on each job — which feeds directly into understanding your profitability without needing a separate spreadsheet.

Common inventory mistakes to avoid

Over-ordering to get a discount. Suppliers often offer tiered pricing — buy 10 screens and get a better unit price than buying 2. This is tempting, but for a solo repairer, buying 10 units of a slow-moving part can tie up hundreds of pounds in stock that sits for months. The cash in your account earning nothing (or funding other purchases) is usually worth more than the marginal saving on unit price. Be disciplined: only buy in bulk for parts you are genuinely confident you will use within four to six weeks.

No record of what was used on each job. If you do not record which parts were used on which repair, you cannot calculate your actual profit per job. You know what you charged. You do not know what you spent to do the repair. Over time, this makes it impossible to know which repairs are genuinely profitable and which you are underpricing.

Mixing OEM and aftermarket without labelling. Aftermarket parts are often physically identical to OEM parts until you install them. If you have both in stock and do not label them clearly, you will eventually fit the wrong one — either charging a customer for OEM and fitting aftermarket (a trust issue) or fitting premium parts when the customer chose the budget option (a margin issue). Label everything clearly at the point it arrives.

Using only one supplier. Single-supplier dependency is a risk. If your main supplier has a stock issue or a delivery delay, you have no fallback. Maintain relationships with at least two reliable suppliers for your most critical parts, and keep their order details somewhere accessible.

Supplier management for a small shop

Two to three reliable suppliers is the right number for most solo repairers. More than that and the administrative overhead of managing different accounts, payment terms, and price lists starts to outweigh the benefit.

For each supplier, keep a note of: their website login, your account number or reference, the specific part numbers for your most common orders, typical delivery times, and your preferred payment method. This information should be somewhere you can access in under a minute — a note in your phone's notes app, a pinned document, or a section of your parts spreadsheet.

When a supplier repeatedly lets you down — delayed deliveries, wrong parts, quality issues — replace them. Good suppliers are part of your operational infrastructure. Bad ones are a hidden cost in time, customer delays, and frustration.

When to upgrade to a proper inventory system

There are clear signals that you have outgrown a lightweight approach:

  • You have multiple technicians using stock and things regularly go missing or miscounted
  • You are ordering from more than three or four suppliers and losing track of what is on order
  • You are buying in bulk and genuinely struggling to know what you have without physically counting
  • You are running multiple locations and need to transfer stock between them

At that point, dedicated inventory software becomes a genuine time-saver rather than an overhead. Until then, simplicity is almost always the right call.

The connection between parts tracking and job profitability

Knowing what you have in stock is only half the value of parts tracking. The other half is understanding your profitability. If you log the parts used against each repair ticket — even just the cost of the main part — you can calculate your actual margin per job rather than estimating it.

This matters more than most new repairers realise. A repair you are charging £60 for might feel profitable until you account for a £30 screen, £5 in consumables, and thirty minutes of labour. Understanding your real numbers on each repair type allows you to price correctly, identify your most and least profitable work, and make smarter decisions about which repairs to focus on.
