How to Manage Repair Tickets Without Losing Your Mind

12 Mar 2026 6 min read
← All articles

A repair ticket is one of the simplest ideas in any service business: a record that a device came in, what the problem is, and what happens to it. In practice, managing repair tickets in a busy shop is where a lot of the chaos lives.

Jobs go missing. Customers call asking for an update and you can't find the ticket. A phone sits in a drawer for three weeks because nobody noticed it was ready. A customer disputes what was agreed and there's no written record to refer to.

This guide walks through the full ticket lifecycle — from the moment a customer walks in to the moment they walk out — and covers what to capture at each stage, what typically goes wrong, and how to keep things under control.

Stage 1: Intake — what you must capture

The intake is the most important part of the process. Everything that happens after relies on what you capture here. Skipping information at intake doesn't save time — it creates problems downstream that take much longer to resolve.

Minimum information every ticket needs

Customer name and phone number. Device make and model. Reported fault (in the customer's words). Pre-existing damage (scratches, cracks, water damage indicators). Agreed price or price range. Estimated turnaround. Any specific instructions or preferences the customer mentioned.

The pre-existing damage section is more important than it sounds. If a customer drops off a phone with a cracked corner and you don't note it, there's a real chance they'll claim you cracked it during the repair. A quick note — even just "corner crack noted at intake" — removes that ambiguity completely.

The agreed price or price range needs to be explicit. "We'll look at it and let you know" is not a price. You need either a fixed quote or a clear diagnostic fee with an agreement about what happens next.

Stage 2: Diagnostics

If the repair requires diagnosis before you can quote a price, that needs to be tracked as its own stage. A ticket sitting in a "needs diagnosis" state is a real status, not an ambiguous middle ground.

Notes from diagnosis matter. What did you find? Is the fault as described, or is there something additional? Is the repair straightforward, or are there complications? These notes protect you if a customer disputes the outcome and help you quote accurately rather than guessing.

If diagnostics reveal a more complex or expensive repair than initially expected, contact the customer before proceeding. Document that you called, what you told them, and what they agreed to. This is one of the most common sources of disputes in repair shops, and written records resolve most of them instantly.

Stage 3: Status updates during the repair

A job should have a clear status at all times. The exact labels vary, but a sensible set looks something like this:

  • Awaiting diagnosis — taken in, not yet looked at
  • Awaiting approval — diagnosed, waiting for customer to confirm quote
  • In progress — repair underway
  • Waiting for parts — ordered, not yet arrived
  • Ready for collection — complete, customer not yet notified or collected
  • Collected — closed, paid

The most overlooked status is "Waiting for parts." Jobs in this state can sit for days or weeks, and it's easy to forget about them. If you're not tracking this explicitly, you'll find yourself surprised when parts arrive and you have to hunt for the original ticket.

Whether you use paper or digital, the rule is the same: if a job's status changes, update the record immediately. Leaving updates until later means they often don't happen.

Stage 4: Customer communication

Most customers don't want to be chased for updates. They want to be notified when their phone is ready and left alone until then. The failure mode on this is twofold: either you don't notify them promptly when the phone is done, or you make them feel like they're constantly chasing you for information you should be providing.

The best practice is simple: notify the customer as soon as the status changes to "ready for collection." Don't wait until the end of the day. Don't assume they'll just call. Send a message — SMS, email, or WhatsApp, whatever they prefer — the moment the job is done.

If a repair is delayed (parts taking longer than expected, a more complex fault than quoted), contact the customer proactively. People are far more forgiving of delays when they're told in advance than when they show up expecting their phone and it's not ready.

Stage 5: Collection and payment

At collection, three things need to happen: the customer confirms the device is theirs, you confirm the repair is complete, and payment is made and recorded.

The confirmation step matters. Ask the customer to check the device in front of you. Does the screen work? Does the button feel right? Are they happy before they leave? This is your last opportunity to address any issue without it becoming a dispute — and a customer who checks the phone in the shop and says "yes, that's fine" has much less ground to stand on if they come back three days later claiming there's a problem.

Record the payment method and amount. If it's cash, note the amount tendered and the change given. This sounds tedious but takes seconds and removes all ambiguity from "I definitely paid" conversations.

Stage 6: Closing the ticket

Once the phone is collected and paid for, the ticket is closed. This sounds obvious, but a lot of shops leave tickets open indefinitely, which means your active job list fills up with completed work and it becomes harder to see what's actually outstanding.

Closing a ticket doesn't mean deleting it. You want to keep historical records — both for your own reference and because customers sometimes come back weeks later about the same device. A closed ticket with good notes is far more useful than a vague memory of what was done.

Common failure modes to watch out for

Beyond individual stages, there are a few systemic problems that affect ticket management in busy shops.

Missing information at intake. The most expensive mistake. Always get the customer's phone number. Always note pre-existing damage. Always confirm the agreed price before they leave.

Tickets sitting uncollected for weeks. If a phone is ready and the customer hasn't collected it after a week, follow up again. After two weeks, consider your policy on uncollected devices — most shops have one, and having it written down means you're not improvising an awkward conversation.

No notes during diagnostic or repair. "Fixed screen" is not a useful note. "Replaced screen — iPhone 14 Pro. OEM part. Tested Face ID, buttons, camera all functioning at collection" is a useful note.

Verbal-only agreements. Anything agreed verbally needs to be in the ticket. "The customer said they were fine with a refurbished screen" is useless if it's not written down and the customer later disputes it.

Digital ticket systems make most of this significantly easier — not because paper is inherently unreliable, but because a digital system can flag overdue jobs, timestamp every change, and make any ticket searchable in seconds. If you're doing more than a handful of repairs a day, it's worth the switch.
