How to Manage Repair Tickets Without Losing Your Mind

28 Feb 2026 5 min read
← All articles

Every repair shop owner has had the same moment: a customer walks in asking about their phone, and for a brief, awful second you're not sure which one is theirs. The ticket is somewhere. The notes are vague. You start mentally replaying what you were working on two days ago. It's a bad feeling — and it gets worse as volume increases.

A solid ticketing system doesn't need to be complicated. It just needs to be consistent. Here's what works.

What a repair ticket actually needs to contain

Strip it back to essentials. Every ticket should capture:

  • Customer name and contact number — so you can call them when the job's done, or if something unexpected comes up
  • Device make, model, and colour — colour matters more than you'd think when two customers hand in the same model
  • IMEI or serial number — critical for identifying the device if a label falls off
  • Fault description — in enough detail that someone else could pick it up and understand what needs doing
  • Agreed price — what you quoted, so there's no ambiguity at collection
  • Estimated turnaround — a rough timeframe you've committed to
  • Ticket reference number — so both you and the customer can refer to it

That's it. Everything else is optional. Some shops add a photo of the device on intake (useful for pre-existing damage), a passcode field (if diagnostics require it), and a consent signature. These are worth adding once your base system is solid.

Common failure modes

Most ticketing problems come down to the same handful of root causes:

Unclear notes. "Screen broken" tells you nothing. "Customer reports screen cracked in top left corner, touch still working, wants cheapest fix option" tells you everything. Train yourself — and any staff — to write notes as if a stranger will be reading them.

Lost tickets. Paper tickets get wet, fall behind benches, or get thrown away by mistake. If you use paper, keep tickets attached to the device using a cable tie tag or a resealable bag. Never leave a ticket floating loose.

No customer contact info. You'd be surprised how many shops miss this. If there's no phone number on the ticket, you can't reach the customer if the repair reveals a new issue, or if a part is delayed. Always take a number, even for quick same-day repairs.

The moment you think "I'll remember that", you've created a future problem. Write it down. Always.

A simple numbering system

Give every ticket a reference number that increments from 1. That's all you need. Don't try to encode dates or device types into the number — it just adds complexity. Ticket #0047 means nothing until you look it up, but it means the same thing to both you and the customer.

When a customer comes to collect, they can give you their number and you can pull up the job instantly. It also means you have a running record of how many jobs you've completed — useful for tracking growth over time.

Status tracking basics

Binary status — "done" or "not done" — is the source of a lot of confusion. Use a simple set of stages instead:

  1. Received — device is in your possession, ticket created
  2. Diagnosing — you're assessing the fault
  3. Waiting for parts — you know what's needed but haven't started the repair yet
  4. In repair — actively being worked on
  5. Testing — repair complete, checking everything works
  6. Ready for collection — notified the customer
  7. Collected — payment taken, device handed back

You don't need all seven stages from day one. Start with Received, In Progress, Ready, and Collected. Add more granularity as your volume grows.

Quick win

When a job moves to "Ready for collection", send the customer a text immediately. Don't batch it up for later — the sooner they collect, the sooner your bench space clears.

Digital vs paper: the honest comparison

Paper has real advantages. It's instant, requires no setup, never crashes, and works without Wi-Fi. Many shops run successfully on paper notebooks for years. If you're doing fewer than 5 repairs a day and work alone, paper may genuinely be all you need.

The problems with paper compound as you scale:

  • Searching for a specific job means flipping through pages
  • There's no way to filter by status — you have to scan everything manually
  • If a notebook goes missing, you've lost your records
  • Staff members can't update a ticket without being physically present
  • There's no payment audit trail — just crossed-out lines

Digital ticketing solves all of these. A well-designed app gives you instant search, status filtering, payment records, and a history that doesn't get lost if someone spills coffee on the counter.

The barrier people cite is usually time to set up. In practice, a good repair shop app takes minutes to get started with — you're not configuring enterprise software.

Keeping tickets under control

A few habits that make a big difference:

Close jobs when they're done. Don't leave completed jobs sitting in your "active" list. Mark them collected as soon as the customer leaves. A clean active list means you can see your real workload at a glance.

Add notes as you work, not after. The habit of writing notes at the end of the day is a bad one. Memory degrades. Add a note the moment something relevant happens: you found additional damage, you called the customer, you ordered a part.

Attach pre-existing damage notes on intake. This is your protection. If a customer collected a phone with a scratch and claims you caused it, your intake note saying "cosmetic scratching on rear panel noted on arrival" is your defence.
